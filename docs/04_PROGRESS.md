- Drafted Chapter 10 — *Engineering Beyond Mechanism* — at 4,312 body words / 4,721 with footnotes. Seven footnotes, all from the verified pool (Ackoff *Re-Creating the Corporation* 1999, Petroski *To Engineer Is Human* 1985, Vincenti *What Engineers Know* 1990, Perrow *Normal Accidents* 1984, Leveson *Engineering a Safer World* 2011, Wiener *Cybernetics* 2nd ed. 1961, Beer *Brain of the Firm* 1972). Structure: a four-paragraph opening on an engineer at a desk meeting a chain of specifications, the part going from her desk through manufacturing and assembly into a system embedded in infrastructure operated by people the engineer would never meet — the chapter's central question pair earned by the example: *does the part meet its specifications?* (the engineer's training) versus *what does the part do to the whole it enters?* (the systems tradition); "From optimisation to design" — Ackoff's distinction between optimisation (the well-posed problem with bounded variables, specified constraints, and a chosen objective function) and design (the problem in which the question of which variables matter is itself part of the work), the engineer's path from one to the other, and the result of the twentieth century's optimisation-heavy training in three concrete classes (machines whose components met specifications and whose interactions destroyed the system, infrastructures whose subsystems were each efficient and whose overall behaviour was unstable, institutions whose departments were each managed competently and whose collective performance worsened); "Petroski on failure" — engineering knowledge advances by failures more reliably than by successes, with the generic-bridge-that-stood-vs-bridge-that-fell motivation followed by the Tacoma Narrows 1940 collapse as the canonical specific case (paraphrased without page-number claims), the discipline of treating failure not as the engineer's shame but as the discipline's most reliable instructor; "Vincenti on what engineers know" — engineering knowledge as a body of design practice rather than applied science, drawn from Vincenti's decade of historical study of US aeronautical engineering between the world wars, with the implication that the engineer's discipline is older than the physics it relies on; "Perrow on the structural inevitability" — Three Mile Island 1979 as Perrow's central case (the small valve failure propagating through tightly-coupled cooling and instrumentation, operators acting reasonably given displays that showed the design's assumptions rather than the plant's state) leading to the *coupling* and *interaction* vocabulary the case earned, and the category of the *normal accident* as the engineering discipline's most important conceptual debt to a sociologist; "Leveson on the absence of control" — STAMP (Systems-Theoretic Accident Model and Processes), the chain-of-events model versus the control-theoretic reformulation, safety as a system property of having the constraints required for safe operation enforced throughout the hierarchical control structure, the accident as control failure rather than chain of component failures, the engineer's competence as control-theoretic as well as component-theoretic; "Wiener and Beer on the engineer's organisation" — Wiener's central observation that the line between organism and machine misplaced the engineer's question, the cybernetic vocabulary's two consequences (engineers could now work on the controlled-feedback-driven-purposive system as the engineering tradition's central case, and the cybernetic vocabulary applied to systems not made of metal — Beer's extension to the design of organisations as engineering objects); "What changes when the engineer asks the systems question" — four differences (the criteria, the collaborations, the failure modes, the knowledge required), the structural inadequacy of the part-only engineer to the configurations the next decades will produce (energy infrastructures, digital networks, autonomous systems, embedded artificial intelligences — typological framing per the 2040-frame discipline); closing on the next decades' best engineers as system designers, the engineers who saw it earliest (Wiener, Beer, Perrow, Leveson) as engineers whose disciplinary positions were not the most prominent of their generations, the engineering object as having changed from the bounded-optimisable-component-decomposable to the emergent-feedback-laden-institutionally-embedded, and the chapter's two questions returning as the question of optimisation and the question of design with the systems tradition's discipline as the discipline of asking the second.
