# Thinking in Wholes — Style Guide

> Inherits from `00_PROJECT_BRIEF.md`. These are rules, not suggestions. When in doubt, follow them. When breaking them, log the break in `03_DECISIONS.md` with reasoning.

---

## Voice — the one paragraph

Calm authority. Unhurried. Adult prose. The voice of someone who has thought about this for thirty years and is not trying to convince anyone — just laying out what they have seen, as carefully as they can, for a reader they respect.

Closer to John Berger or Marilynne Robinson than to a TED talk. Closer to E. F. Schumacher than to Malcolm Gladwell. Closer to Wendell Berry than to Tim Ferriss.

---

## Sentence-level rules

**Variable length.** Some short. Some that breathe across multiple clauses without becoming labyrinthine. Avoid uniform rhythm — uniform rhythm reads as performed.

**Concrete before abstract.** Every abstraction earns its place by appearing after the example that motivated it, not before. "The appendix has no effect on the body" before "membership in a system is functional, not spatial." Never the reverse.

**Specificity over generality.** "Ackoff in 1971" beats "a thinker once said." "The 142-car thought experiment" beats "a famous example." Names, dates, places, and the specific texture of the example.

**Earn every adverb.** "Carefully" must do work. If it doesn't, cut it.

**No exclamation points.** None. Anywhere.

**No rhetorical questions** unless they are immediately answered with substance.

## Words and phrases that do not appear in this book

These are management-consultant tells, TED-talk tells, or self-help tells. Each one signals a thinking style the book opposes.

| Forbidden | Why |
|---|---|
| "leverage" (as a verb) | consultant slop |
| "synergy" / "synergistic" | empty |
| "in essence" | usually filler |
| "at its core" | filler |

| "navigate" (as a metaphor) | overused beyond meaning |

## What the book embraces

- **Primary source engagement.** Quote sparingly and accurately, with full citation. Read what Ackoff or Meadows actually wrote, not summaries of summaries.
- **Concrete examples that survive translation.** The car. The appendix. The acorn. Examples should illustrate something general while being themselves specific and visualizable.
- **Naming complexity.** When something is genuinely hard or contested, say so. Do not pretend to certainty the author does not have.
- **Willingness to be wrong on the record.** Predictions are made with dates attached. The 2040 reader can check them.
- **Acknowledged blind spots.** Better to say "I cannot see this clearly" than to fake confidence.
- **Long sentences when the thought requires them.** Not every idea fits in twelve words. Berger and Robinson use long sentences. So can we.

## Oxford referencing — the citation system

The book uses **Oxford referencing** (also known as the Oxford notes-and-bibliography system, or "documentary-note style"). This is the system used by historians and many UK academic publishers. It pairs **footnotes** in the text with a **bibliography** at the end.

### Footnote — first reference to a work

Full citation. Format:

> First Name Last Name, *Title of Work* (Place of Publication: Publisher, Year), page number.

**Examples:**

> ¹ Donella H. Meadows, *Thinking in Systems: A Primer* (White River Junction, VT: Chelsea Green, 2008), 25.

> ² Russell L. Ackoff, *Re-Creating the Corporation: A Design of Organizations for the 21st Century* (New York: Oxford University Press, 1999), 47.

> ³ Christopher Alexander, *The Timeless Way of Building* (New York: Oxford University Press, 1979), 125–127.

### Footnote — subsequent references to the same work

Short form. Author last name + short title + page.

> ⁴ Meadows, *Thinking in Systems*, 31.

> ⁵ Ackoff, *Re-Creating the Corporation*, 89.

If the citation is **immediately preceding** (same source as last note), use *ibid*.:

> ⁶ Ibid., 33.

(Use *ibid.* sparingly; readers find it disorienting at scale. When in doubt, use the short form.)

### Footnote — journal article

> First Name Last Name, "Title of Article," *Journal Name* volume, no. issue (Year): page range, page cited.

> ⁷ Donella H. Meadows, "Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System," *Whole Earth* 91 (1997): 78–84, 80.

### Footnote — chapter in edited volume

> First Name Last Name, "Title of Chapter," in *Title of Volume*, ed. Editor Name (Place: Publisher, Year), page.

### Footnote — direct quotation

When quoting directly, the page number is mandatory. Quotations longer than three lines are set as block quotes (indented, no quotation marks); shorter quotations run inline with double quotation marks.

### Bibliography — at end of book

Alphabetical by author last name. Format slightly different from footnote (last name first, period delimiters):

> Ackoff, Russell L. *Re-Creating the Corporation: A Design of Organizations for the 21st Century*. New York: Oxford University Press, 1999.

> Meadows, Donella H. *Thinking in Systems: A Primer*. White River Junction, VT: Chelsea Green, 2008.

> Meadows, Donella H. "Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System." *Whole Earth* 91 (1997): 78–84.

### In-text mechanics

- Footnote markers are **superscript Arabic numerals**: ¹ ² ³
- Footnote marker comes **after** punctuation: "...the appendix has no function in the body."¹
- For block quotes, footnote marker comes after the closing punctuation of the quote.
- Footnote numbering runs **continuously through the chapter** (not the whole book), restarting at 1 in each new chapter.

### Hard rules

- **Every direct quotation has a page number.** No exceptions. If we don't have the page, we don't quote it.
- **Every claim attributed to a named author is footnoted on first use in a chapter.** Common knowledge does not need a citation; specific arguments do.
- **No citation is invented.** If we don't have a verified source, we don't make the claim.
- **First citation in chapter is full; subsequent are short.** This is the discipline of the system.
- **Bibliography reflects the footnotes exactly.** Every work cited in any footnote appears in the bibliography. No bibliography ghosts.

## The 2040 frame — operational rules

These are derived from the brief but worth restating as prose-level rules:

- No reference to a specific company unless the company has existed for 75+ years and will plausibly exist in 2040 (e.g., Toyota is fine; a 2024 AI startup is not).
- No reference to a specific political figure unless they are dead and historically significant.
- No reference to a specific contemporary controversy by name. The pattern is fair game; the news cycle is not.
- AI is referred to as a class of technology, not by lab or model name.
- Crises (climate, inequality, institutional collapse) are named but not anchored to specific 2026 events.
- The reader is addressed as "you" sparingly. They are addressed as a thinking adult.

If a sentence would feel dated by 2032, it does not survive the next pass.

---

## Bullet points and lists in the body

The body of the book — the prose — does not use bullet-point lists. Bullets fragment thought. They are appropriate in a reference work, not in an argument.

Exceptions:
- The glossary (it is a reference)
- The references and bibliography (they are reference)
- Genuine enumerations the reader will return to (e.g., the five practices in Chapter 13, but presented as a numbered enumeration with full prose treatment of each, not bullet points).

---

## On metaphor

Use metaphor sparingly and concretely. A metaphor must be visualizable. Mixed metaphors are forbidden. Dead metaphors ("the cutting edge," "the bottom line") are forbidden because they are no longer metaphors — they are placeholders for thought.

When a chapter has a guiding metaphor (the wound, the loop, the inheritance), it must hold across the chapter without becoming labored.

---

## Length discipline

A chapter that runs short is honest. A chapter that runs long has not been edited. The 4,500-word target is a target. A chapter at 3,800 that says everything it needs to say is finished. A chapter at 5,800 that needs cutting will be cut.

---

## What "good" looks like — a touchstone

When uncertain whether a passage is working, ask: would Marilynne Robinson have allowed this sentence into *The Givenness of Things*? Would John Berger have allowed it into *Ways of Seeing*? Would Donella Meadows have allowed it into *Thinking in Systems*?

If the answer is no, the sentence is not finished.
