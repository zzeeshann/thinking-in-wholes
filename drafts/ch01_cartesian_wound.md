# Chapter 1 — The Cartesian Wound

The method is older than any of us. Three and a half centuries ago, a young Frenchman named René Descartes spent a winter alone in what he called a *poêle* — a stove-heated room — somewhere in southern Germany, and resolved that he would no longer accept any received opinion he had not examined for himself. The conviction matured slowly. Eighteen years later, in 1637, he published a small French book called *Discourse on the Method of Rightly Conducting One's Reason and of Seeking Truth in the Sciences*, set in the back of a larger volume containing his work on optics, geometry, and meteorology, more or less as a preface to the technical work it served.[^1] In Part Two of the *Discourse*, he set down four rules for the conduct of thought. The first was to accept nothing as true that did not present itself with the clarity that excluded doubt. The third was to ascend from the simplest objects to the most complex. The fourth was to make enumerations so complete and reviews so general that nothing could be left out. The second rule — divide each problem into as many parts as possible, and treat each part separately — is the one we have lived inside ever since.

What Descartes was breaking from is now hard to feel. The European universities of his century taught a thousand-year-old synthesis of Aristotle and the Church Fathers, in which questions about nature, ethics, theology, and metaphysics were braided together so tightly that to ask about one was to be obliged to all of them. A serious investigation of the motion of a falling stone could not proceed without first establishing the stone's place in a hierarchy of substances, the soul of which it was a faint image, the will of God by which it descended, and the sin from which the descent took its character. The method was synthetic to the point of paralysis. Almost nothing useful could be settled, because almost nothing could be cleanly held apart. Descartes' four rules are the announcement of a permission that did not previously exist: the permission to study one thing at a time, by itself, without reference to the others, until the parts had been understood and could be reassembled.

The permission worked. A generation after Descartes' death, Isaac Newton applied it to nature itself. The *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* was printed in London in 1687 by Joseph Streater for the Royal Society of London, and reorganised the study of physical nature around four divisions — mass, force, distance, and time — whose interactions Newton showed to be governed by mathematical laws of unprecedented generality.[^2] The motion of a falling apple and the motion of the moon were the same kind of motion, decomposable into the same parts, and described by the same equation. The cosmos, on Newton's account, was a system of inert bodies acted upon by quantifiable forces. The clock — a machine of separable parts whose interaction is wholly described by their geometry — was no longer a metaphor for the universe. It was, very nearly, a model of it.

This was the most consequential act of intellectual confidence in the modern era. For two centuries after the *Principia*, every advance in the natural sciences was understood as the further application of the same method to the same kind of problem. Ships could be sailed by it; bridges could be built by it; eclipses could be predicted; the orbits of planets and the trajectories of cannonballs could be calculated to ten decimal places. The success was so total that "scientific" and "Cartesian-Newtonian" became, for working purposes, the same word. A serious investigation, in any field, was understood to require the dividing of the field into elementary objects, the discovery of laws governing their interaction, and the prediction of their behaviour from those laws. Anything that could not be approached this way was not yet science.

The *Principia* was also the moment at which the method left the philosopher's study and entered an institution. The Royal Society of London, founded a quarter century before the book's publication, treated Newton's work as a vindication of its founding programme: nature was to be investigated by experiment, mathematised where possible, and reported in plain English to a public of educated readers. The book itself was difficult — its proofs proceed in a Euclidean style that almost no one could follow without years of training — but the picture of the world it offered was easily summarised, and the summary travelled. By the middle of the eighteenth century, "Newtonian" had become a synonym for "rigorous"; by the end of the eighteenth, the cathedrals of European cities had ceased to be the buildings to which serious thinkers reported. The serious thinkers reported to laboratories, observatories, and learned societies, all of which were organised around the principle that to understand a phenomenon was to break it into measurable components. The institutional revolution was as consequential as the intellectual one. A method now had a building.

A century after Newton, the method moved from nature to society. Adam Smith's *Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*, published in London in 1776 by W. Strahan and T. Cadell, opens with a small manufactory of pins.[^3] A workman alone, Smith observes — untrained in this particular trade and using only the tools to hand — could perhaps make one pin in a day, and certainly could not make twenty. But by dividing the work into eighteen distinct operations, with one man drawing the wire, another straightening it, a third cutting it, a fourth pointing it, and so on through grinding and polishing and head-fitting and packaging, ten workmen between them could produce upwards of forty-eight thousand pins in the same day. The increase was not in human strength or skill; the workmen were the same workmen. It was in the geometry of the work. Descartes' rule of analysis, applied to the labour of human beings, produced a result no one in the previous five thousand years of human production had imagined possible.

Smith was a moral philosopher, not a publicist of factories; the *Wealth of Nations* is at least as much a book about ethics as it is about economics. He saw, more clearly than most of those who came after him, that the division of labour was a method whose costs would have to be reckoned with as well as its benefits, and warned in Book V of the *Wealth of Nations* that the worker confined to a single repetitive operation would lose the moral and intellectual capacities that made him fully a human being. The same Smith who described the pin factory in Book I as the source of unprecedented wealth described it in Book V as the source of an unprecedented stupefaction; both passages are written in the same calm prose, by the same hand, with no apparent sense of contradiction. He understood that his country was about to commit itself to a method of work that would deliver the goods and damage the workers, and he did not pretend the trade was free.

The warning was filed and the method was adopted. Within a hundred and fifty years, the population of the earth had quadrupled and the average human lifespan had nearly doubled, and most of that lift was the lift the pin factory promised: take the work apart, recombine it, multiply the output. The factory replaced the workshop; the wage replaced the apprenticeship; the city replaced the village; the consumer replaced the householder; the firm replaced the trade. The Cartesian rule was no longer a method for thinking. It was a method for organising economic life, and through that, the lives of nearly everyone alive. By the end of the nineteenth century, almost no one in the industrialising world worked at a job that an eighteenth-century artisan would have recognised. The work itself had been disassembled and reassembled around the principle of the second rule.

A further century, and the method moved from labour as such to the labouring body. Frederick Winslow Taylor was a Pennsylvania engineer who began his working life as a labourer in a steel works and ended it as the most influential industrial consultant of his generation. *The Principles of Scientific Management*, published in New York in 1911 by Harper and Brothers, was the third application of Descartes' rule — this time to the worker himself.[^4] The method that Newton had used to analyse motion, and Smith had used to analyse production, Taylor used to analyse the human movements that made production possible. The work of a labourer was decomposed, with stopwatch in hand, into elementary motions; each motion was studied for its time, its energy cost, its waste; an optimal procedure was constructed; the procedure was taught back to the worker as the new way to do the job. Taylor called this the "one best way." Robert Kanigel's biography, the standard treatment, uses the phrase as its title.[^5]

What Taylor's analysis did to manual labour was not new in kind — it was the same method Smith had described — but it was new in intensity. Smith had divided the work; Taylor divided the worker. Where the pin factory had assigned each man one operation, the stopwatch assigned each man's left hand a duration measured in tenths of a second. The work of the body became a sequence of observable units, separable, measurable, optimisable. The factory floor became a laboratory.

Taylor met fierce resistance from the workers he studied. He was unpopular among labourers, who recognised in his method an extension of management's authority into the most private regions of bodily skill, and he was unpopular among many managers, who found his consultancy fees high and his manner imperious. The resistance was telling: it was the resistance of human beings to being analysed in the way one would analyse the action of a piston. Taylor was called before a special committee of the United States House of Representatives in 1912 to defend his methods, after a strike at the Watertown Arsenal had been blamed on his system; his testimony, expanded and revised, became the basis of further publications. The system survived the controversy. The productivity gains were too large to ignore, and within a decade scientific management had been adopted across American industry, then European, then — in one of the more arresting marriages of method and ideology in twentieth-century history — by the Soviet Union, where Lenin praised Taylorism as a scientific tool that the proletarian state should master and deploy. The same method Adam Smith had described as the engine of capitalism became, in the 1920s, the operating principle of revolutionary socialism. The method was older than the politics it served.

Henry Ford, who had read Taylor and corresponded with associates of his, carried the analysis one step further. Where Taylor had broken the work down to its motions, Ford broke the workspace itself down to its sequence. The assembly line — a moving conveyor along which a partially-built object travels past stations of specialists, each performing one decomposed operation — is the Cartesian method made physical. The method that began as a rule for thinking, in a stove-heated German room near the start of the seventeenth century, had become by the start of the twentieth a rule for arranging buildings and the bodies inside them. By 1913, when Ford's first moving assembly line at Highland Park, Michigan, reduced the assembly time of a Model T chassis from twelve and a half hours to ninety-three minutes, the rule had taken over the structure of work itself. Ford's offer of five dollars a day to workers willing to submit to the line — roughly twice the prevailing wage — was not a generosity but a compensation: the work had become so reductive, so unrelenting, so far removed from any practice that a craftsman would have recognised, that even an ample wage was needed to keep men on the line. The line was efficient. The men hated it. Both facts were true at once, and the truth of the first was used to override the truth of the second for the next half-century.

By the early twentieth century, then, Descartes' second rule had moved through four great applications. From thought (Descartes) to nature (Newton) to society (Smith) to the labouring body (Taylor and Ford). At each step the method had been applied to a more concrete object, and at each step it had produced more material output than its predecessors had thought possible. The Cartesian method was no longer a tool of the intellect. It was the operating principle of the modern world.

The success of the method, across these three and a half centuries, has to be honoured before its limits can be named. Take any one of the things a developed twenty-first-century life takes for granted, and trace it back. The clock that runs reliably in the pocket. The aeroplane that crosses oceans. The medicine that cures a disease that would have killed a great-grandparent. The road that holds, the bridge that stands, the dam that controls a river. The flush toilet that prevents cholera. The vaccine, the X-ray, the antibiotic, the elevator. None of these came from holism. All came from analysis. Each was made possible by isolating a phenomenon, studying it as though it were separable from everything around it, optimising the part, and reintegrating it into the whole as a piece. The Cartesian method built almost everything we mean by "the modern world." It is the most successful intellectual operation in human history.

Anyone who proposes to criticise the method has to begin by acknowledging this. The systems thinkers who came after, in the second half of the twentieth century, were not opposed to analysis. They were opposed to its monopoly. The error they sought to name was not Descartes' but the error his followers had eventually slipped into: the conviction that because the method works on so many things, it must work on everything; that because the parts of a clock can be studied separately, the parts of every kind of system must be too; that because a watch comes apart cleanly, the world it describes will also.

A method that has never failed begins to look like a description of reality. This is the trap, and it is hard to overstate how completely it took hold. Three centuries of overwhelming success made the Cartesian rule almost invisible: it stopped looking like a method — a particular tool with a particular range — and started looking like a true picture of how the world is. The world, on this picture, comes in parts. The parts are real; the whole is the sum of them. To understand a thing is to take it apart and study its pieces. To improve a thing is to improve each piece separately. To organise a society is to give each piece its own department, its own budget, and its own measurable target.

Once the picture took hold, it produced its own kind of damage, and that damage is what most of this book is about. Schools were organised by subject; subjects were optimised; the children, who are not subjects but persons, came out of school knowing pieces of disciplines without knowing what to do with their lives. Hospitals were organised by department; departments were optimised; the patients, who are not collections of departments but bodies inside lives, came out of hospitals having been treated for diseases without having been seen. Companies were organised by function; functions were optimised; the company, which is not a sum of functions but an enterprise, regularly hit every quarterly target on the way to its own failure. Cities were organised by sector — transportation, sanitation, housing, employment, education — and each sector was managed by a separate office reporting to a separate authority, and the inhabitants, who are not sums of sectors but lives, lived inside cities that no one had ever designed as places to live.

Even the universities, which had been the original site of the synthetic medieval learning that Descartes was reacting against, eventually reorganised themselves on the Cartesian principle. The modern academic department — biology in one building, economics in another, philosophy in a third — is the institutional form of analysis applied to knowledge itself. The student who wishes to understand what a city is must take separate courses in urban planning, sociology, transportation engineering, real estate finance, and the history of architecture, each taught by a person who reports to a different chair, each examined separately, each footnoting its own literature, none integrated by anyone before they reach the student's mind. The university was the last institution to make peace with the Cartesian rule, and when it did, it did so completely. By the late twentieth century, almost no major university maintained any office responsible for the coherence of a student's education across departments. The departments existed; the coherence was the student's problem.

Governments organised themselves the same way. A modern state has a ministry of health, a ministry of education, a ministry of agriculture, a ministry of defence, a ministry of finance, and depending on its size and ambitions, a dozen or two more. Each ministry has its own minister, its own civil service, its own budget, its own targets, and its own history of reform. The number of issues that lie cleanly inside the remit of a single ministry is small. The number that lie across several is most of what governments are now asked to address — the health of an urban population, for example, depends on housing, employment, transportation, food supply, education, the quality of the water and the air, the structure of family life, the availability of clean fuel, and the regulation of the substances people are sold for entertainment, none of which is in the remit of the Ministry of Health. The ministries optimise their parts. The whole, which is the population, takes whatever the parts add up to, and adapts as it can.

Even the household budget, in many places, has been reorganised along the same lines. A family with several earners and several outgoings is now routinely advised to track its expenditures by category — housing, transport, food, entertainment, savings — and to optimise each category against a benchmark, on the model of a small enterprise. The advice is sensible up to a point and corrosive past it. A family is not a small enterprise. The categories interact: the cheaper neighbourhood requires the longer commute requires the worse food requires the diminished health requires the additional medical expense, and the savings target that motivated the move is, two years on, no longer met. To organise the family by category is to lose the family. But the categorisation is so deeply assumed that to suggest otherwise is to be heard as financially irresponsible.

This is not a moral failure. It is an intellectual one. The method that produced the steam engine and the antibiotic, the aeroplane and the silicon chip, was applied past its competence to objects that did not behave as steam engines behave. A steam engine is a system of separable parts whose interaction is geometric: each part can be improved on its own bench and reinstalled, and the engine will work better. A child is not. A patient is not. A company is not. A city is not. These are wholes whose properties live in the connections, not in the parts; the connections cannot be improved on a separate bench because they cannot be removed and put back. To analyse them as one would analyse a steam engine is to dismantle them. The method works perfectly, and the patient dies.

Fritjof Capra, writing in 1982, called the situation a crisis of perception.[^6] He meant that the most pressing problems of the late twentieth century — environmental, economic, social — were systemic problems being addressed with a parts-based vocabulary that could not name what was happening. The vocabulary was not stupid; it was the vocabulary that had built the modern world. It was simply being used past the point at which it remained competent. Half a century after Capra wrote, the crisis of perception is no longer the prelude to the problem. It is the problem. The institutions that organise public life have become so deeply Cartesian in their structure that they cannot easily perceive the failures their own structure produces. The damage now visible — the institutions optimised into incoherence, the cures that worsen the disease, the economies that grow themselves out of the conditions of their own continuance — is what the Cartesian method looks like when it is applied to objects that do not yield to it.

It would be unfair to Descartes to say he was wrong. He was answering a question his century needed answered, and he answered it with a brilliance that shaped every century after. What he said was true. What he did not say was that the method had a range, and that the range had an outside. He could not have said it, because the problems his century faced did not yet require it. The problems of 2026 do.

The method, properly understood, is one of two great operations of thought. Its complement is synthesis — the ascent from the parts to the whole, the recognition of properties that exist in the connections and not in the components. The systems thinkers who appear in the next chapter, and who will be the recurring company of this book, are the people who, over the second half of the twentieth century, took on the work of restoring synthesis to a culture that had forgotten it. They did not throw out analysis. They could not have; analysis is too good. What they tried to do was return it to its proper scale — one operation among others, useful for some classes of problem and useless or actively harmful for others. The discipline they were proposing is that, before applying analysis to a problem, one has first to ask whether the problem is of the kind that yields to analysis. If it is a clock, take it apart. If it is a child, do not.

This is the discipline the rest of the book is concerned with. It does not require the rejection of three and a half centuries of work. It requires only that the work be recognised for what it was — a method, brilliantly developed, magnificently applied, and never supplemented with what it always lacked.

The wound, in the end, is not Descartes' fault. The wound is the method outliving its competence. We have inherited a tool that builds bridges and breaks lives, and we have not yet learned to put it down when the work in front of us is a life.
