# Chapter 8 — The Maker's Dilemma

A novelist has written a sentence. It is a fine sentence — finer than anything else in the chapter it occupies, perhaps finer than anything else in the book. She has worked on it for months. It is the kind of sentence that, once it arrives, justifies the previous year's labour. She reads it aloud to herself in the empty room, and it stands.

The chapter the sentence belongs to, however, is not as good as the sentence. The chapter does what the book asks of it; it does not do anything more. The sentence, sitting inside the chapter, draws attention to itself in a way the chapter cannot afford. The reader who has been following the book's argument is, in the moment of the sentence, suddenly aware that she is in the presence of writing rather than thinking. The sentence is doing what the book did not need a sentence to do. It is not load-bearing for the book's argument; it is load-bearing only for itself.

The discipline is to cut it. The novelist does not cut it. The book is published. Reviewers, predictably, single out the sentence for praise. The book as a whole is reviewed politely. Years later, looking at the book on the shelf, the novelist is able to say what the reviews could not: the sentence is what made the book not as good as it could have been. The book lost its centre at the moment the sentence took it.

This is not a story about a writer who has bad taste. It is a story about a writer whose taste was, for that moment, operating on the wrong scale. The sentence was beautiful at the level of the sentence. It was wrong at the level of the chapter, and it was disastrous at the level of the book. The taste that recognised the sentence's beauty was not equipped to ask what the sentence was doing to the work it had been written into. The author had been, for the duration of the chapter's life, the wrong size of mind for her own book.

This is the maker's dilemma. It is not a dilemma about whether to make the part beautiful; the part should be made as beautiful as the maker can make it. It is a dilemma about what to do with the part once it is beautiful, when the work it belongs to cannot afford the beauty. The dilemma occurs at every scale at which the maker is working: the sentence inside the paragraph, the paragraph inside the chapter, the chapter inside the book; the bar inside the phrase, the phrase inside the movement, the movement inside the symphony; the shot inside the scene, the scene inside the film. At every level, the maker's first task is to make the part as well as the part can be made. The maker's second task — the harder task, the task most creative failure is a failure of — is to subordinate the part to the work.

## What the systems tradition has been describing all along

The systems literature has a name for this. It is not the name the writer would use. It is the name the engineer would use, but it points at what the writer is doing.

A part of a system, in the systems-tradition vocabulary, has properties — the things that can be said about the part considered on its own — and it has functions, which are the things the part does for the whole the part belongs to. The two are not the same. A heart, considered on its own, has properties: a particular weight, a particular musculature, a characteristic rhythm. A heart, considered inside a body, has a function: it circulates the blood. Both are true of the same heart, but the question that matters depends on the scale at which one is asking. A pathologist asks about properties. A cardiologist asks about function. The pathologist's question, asked of a beating heart, is the wrong question.

Russell Ackoff, who built his career on this distinction, kept a thought experiment for use in his lectures. He would ask his audience to imagine collecting the best part from each of a hundred and forty-two cars: the engine of one, the transmission of another, the brakes of a third, and so on through the catalogue. He would ask whether the resulting assembly, made of the best parts of every car on earth, would work. The audience always said no. The reason it would not work was the reason the heart's properties are not its function. The parts had each been optimised inside their respective wholes; what made each part the best of its kind was its fit with the rest of its original car; assembled together, the parts would not fit with each other. The whole was not the sum of the best parts. The whole was the configuration in which the parts could perform their functions. A part that performed brilliantly in isolation was, in most cases, the part that performed worst in combination.[^1]

The maker who cannot cut the beautiful sentence is in the same position as the engineer who cannot subordinate the engine to the car the engine has to fit. The sentence is a part. The chapter is the whole the part has to fit. The part's properties — its rhythm, its precision, its rightness on the page — are not its function. Its function is what it does for the chapter. A sentence that calls attention to its own beauty has stopped functioning as a sentence and has begun functioning as an exhibit. The maker who allows this has stopped making the chapter and has begun curating the maker's own work.

The discipline of subordination, named by the systems tradition as the structural commitment that separates good design from spectacular waste, is the same discipline a maker has been asked for since the form was invented. The maker's vocabulary is different. The verb is rarely *subordinate*. It is more often *cut*, *let go*, *kill the darling*, *serve the work*. The verb does not matter; the discipline does. Every time a maker has chosen the work over the maker's love for the part the work cannot bear, the maker has performed the act the systems tradition has spent half a century giving a vocabulary to.

## Tolstoy on what the work is for

Tolstoy, in his late critical writings, gave the most uncompromising account of what art was for, and therefore of what art's subordination was to. The account is moralistic, and it has been resisted on those grounds for a hundred and twenty years. The moralism is not what is interesting about the account. What is interesting is the structural claim underneath the moralism: that art is not a thing but a transmission, that the work an artist makes is the medium by which a feeling that occurred in the artist is communicated to the people who receive the work, and that the artist's responsibility is therefore to the transmission, not to the artefact.[^2]

A work that demonstrates the artist's skill but does not transmit the feeling, on Tolstoy's account, is not art; it is craft displayed for craft's sake. A work that transmits the feeling without demonstrating skill is art; it has done what the work was for. The hierarchy is inverted from the conventional one. The skilful artefact is not the goal; the artefact is the means. The feeling — the one the artist was inside, the one the work was made to carry — is the whole the artefact serves.

The implication, for the maker, is severe. Every part of the work is to be assessed not for what it is on its own but for what it does to the transmission. The brilliant passage that interrupts the transmission is not a successful part of the work; it is a failure. The understated passage that preserves the transmission, even at the cost of looking ordinary on the page, is a successful part of the work. The reader's experience — the feeling the reader is to come away with — is the whole the parts must subordinate themselves to. The artist who cannot do this has, on Tolstoy's account, mistaken the artefact for the work.

The argument has been resisted because Tolstoy's account of which feelings count as the right ones to transmit was narrow and, by the standards of his successors, often wrong. But the structural claim — that the work is the transmission, not the artefact, and that the parts of the artefact must subordinate themselves to the transmission — survives the resistance. It is the claim every editor has made to every author whose chapter is too clever for the book it belongs to. It is the claim every conductor has made to every soloist whose phrasing is too prominent for the symphony. It is the claim every filmmaker has made about the scene that has to go.

## Berger on the frame

Berger, who wrote about looking with a precision the discipline rarely received before him, was making a related point at a different scale. The work of art, on Berger's account, is never autonomous. It is always inside a frame, and the frame is always inside another frame, and the meaning of the work depends on the framing as much as on the work itself.[^3]

The frame Berger had most often in mind was the social and historical one. A painting hanging in a museum is, on his treatment, a different work from the same painting reproduced in a magazine, or the same painting on a postcard sold in the museum's gift shop. The image is the same; the work is not. What the work is depends on what frame the viewer is inside when they encounter the image. The painter who imagines the work in the museum and the painter who imagines the work on the postcard are not painting the same thing. The painter who refuses to imagine either is leaving the determination of the work's meaning to the institutions that will frame it.

Berger's claim, applied to the maker's dilemma, is that the work is never the artefact alone. The work is the artefact inside the receiving conditions. A sentence is not the sentence; it is the sentence inside the paragraph that surrounds it, inside the chapter, inside the book, inside the cultural moment in which the book is read. The artist who attends only to the sentence is producing for a frame that does not exist. The frame the work will arrive inside has its own demands, and the work must meet them.

The maker's dilemma takes a particular form, in Berger's framing, because the maker has access to the inner frame and not to the outer one. The maker controls the paragraph the sentence is inside, and the chapter, and the book; the maker does not control the cultural moment, the reader's mood, the surrounding works the reader has read recently.[^4] The discipline is to make the inner frames as right as they can be, on the recognition that the outer frames will impose their own meaning regardless. A part that fits the inner frames will, in most cases, also fit the outer ones; a part that has been written for itself, free of any frame, will fit no frame.

## Le Guin on craft as service

Ursula K. Le Guin's late writings on craft, gathered most patiently in *Steering the Craft* and *The Wave in the Mind*, are the most useful available treatment of how the maker's discipline of subordination is actually practised, sentence by sentence. Le Guin was unsentimental about craft. She did not treat craft as the magical attribute that distinguished real artists from amateurs; she treated craft as a body of trainable skills whose function was to make the writer transparent to the work. The writer who has mastered her sentences, on Le Guin's treatment, is not the writer whose sentences sing the loudest; she is the writer whose sentences cease to interrupt the reader's attention to what is being said.[^5]

The practical advice in *Steering the Craft* is therefore consistently in the direction of subordination. The exercises ask the writer to attend to particular elements of the sentence — point of view, sentence length, repetition, the rhythm of the paragraph — not as ends in themselves but as instruments to be tuned to the work. A sentence is to be assessed by what it does in the paragraph, not by how it reads aloud on its own. A paragraph is to be assessed by what it does in the chapter. The writer who follows the exercises will find, after enough years, that the sentences she is most pleased with are typically the ones whose pleasure is hardest to locate when one looks for it on the line. The line has dissolved into the paragraph. The paragraph has dissolved into the chapter. What remains is the chapter, which the reader experiences as continuous attention rather than as a series of admirable sentences.

The maker's dilemma, on Le Guin's treatment, is therefore most acute at the level of the writer's pride.[^6] The pride wants the sentence to be admirable. The work needs the sentence to be invisible. The discipline is to choose the work. The choice is harder for the maker who is good than for the maker who is bad, because the good maker is producing the kinds of sentences that are, in fact, admirable, and the temptation to leave them in the foreground is correspondingly strong. The discipline of subordination, on Le Guin's treatment, is what most reliably distinguishes the writer who has been writing for thirty years from the writer who has been writing for ten.

## Robinson on the receiving end

Marilynne Robinson's late essays are themselves an example of what Le Guin recommended. Robinson's prose is famous for its weight, but the weight is not in any individual sentence; it is in the accumulation of attention across a paragraph or a page. Read on the line, the prose is plain. Read across the paragraph, it is impossible. The plainness on the line is the discipline that produces the impossibility on the paragraph. The reader, finishing one of Robinson's pages, is unable to point to the sentence that produced the effect; the effect is produced by the way the sentences yield to one another, refusing to detain the reader's attention even as they accumulate the attention's full weight.[^7]

Robinson, who has written with care about the discipline of essay-writing, has been explicit about what the work asks of the writer. The essay, for her, is the form in which a thought is permitted to develop at the pace of its own discovery. The writer's job is to follow the thought; the writer's discipline is to refuse to interrupt the thought with the writer's display. The display is what the essay rejects. What the essay receives, in compensation for the display the writer foregoes, is the reader's confidence that the essay is going somewhere the writer has not yet been. That confidence is impossible if the writer has been performing along the way.

The implication, for the maker's dilemma, is that the discipline of subordination is not a sacrifice. The maker who learns to subordinate the part is not surrendering anything the maker would otherwise have had. The work the maker can do, with the part subordinated, is a different work — a deeper one, capable of receiving the reader's full attention rather than competing with it. What the maker gives up is the small pleasure of the part well-done; what the maker receives is the larger pleasure of the work well-done. The trade is favourable. The difficulty is that the small pleasure is available immediately, on the page, and the larger pleasure is available only on completion of the whole, which is always months or years away, and is never as visible as the sentence that has just been written.

## Lewis on what reading needs

C. S. Lewis, in *An Experiment in Criticism*, took up the same problem from the receiving end. The reader, on Lewis's account, is not a passive consumer of the artefact; the reader is engaged in a particular act, the act of receiving the work, and the act has its own conditions. A reader who has been trained to admire the part — to recognise the brilliant sentence, the well-shaped chapter, the cunning device — has been trained, by Lewis's lights, in the wrong discipline. The right discipline is to receive the work; to surrender, for the duration of the reading, to what the work is doing; to allow the work to do what it has set out to do, without insisting on the reader's continuing presence as a judge.[^8]

The reader who reads in this way is, on Lewis's account, the reader the maker should have been imagining. The maker who imagines the part-admiring reader will write for that reader; the maker who imagines the work-receiving reader will write for that one. The two readers want different things from the page. The first wants to be impressed; the second wants to be carried. The work that impresses interrupts; the work that carries does not. The maker's choice of imagined reader is, in this sense, a choice about what the work is for, and therefore a choice about which parts can be allowed to remain.

## Why the discipline is hard

The discipline is hard for reasons that are particular to making. Three of them are worth naming.

The first is that the part is what the maker has access to in the moment. The maker writing the sentence is inside the sentence; the chapter is, at that moment, an abstraction. The chapter exists, in the maker's mind, as an outline, a memory, an intention. The sentence exists on the page. The maker's attention is therefore drawn, by the structure of the work, to the level at which the work is currently being made, and only with effort to the level the work belongs to. The discipline of subordination requires the maker to hold the higher level in mind while attending to the lower. This is not what cognition does naturally.

The second is that the part is what the maker has worked on. The hours that produced the sentence are the hours the maker remembers. The hours that will be required to integrate the sentence into the chapter are hours the maker has not yet spent. The maker's attachment to the sentence reflects the work that has gone into it; that attachment has no counterpart for the chapter, which has not yet been completed and into which no comparable hours have been poured. The cost of cutting the sentence is therefore vivid; the benefit of cutting it is abstract. Most makers, most of the time, choose the vivid over the abstract.

The third is that the part is what the maker is praised for. Reviewers cite sentences. Listeners hum melodies. Audiences quote scenes. The whole — the book that turns inside the reader's head, the symphony that builds across an evening, the film that organises memory afterwards — is what the maker has actually made, but it is not what the maker is most reliably credited for. The institutions of reception are calibrated to the part. The maker who serves the whole is therefore serving a thing that will not be visible to the maker's reception in the way the part will. To choose the whole, against the part, is to choose against the institutions one is making for. This is hard, and it gets harder the more the maker has been rewarded by the institutions for choosing the part.

## What the discipline looks like in practice

The discipline of subordination is rarely taught directly. It is acquired, in the experience of every maker who has acquired it, through a sequence of editorial relationships in which the maker has been helped to see what the work needed and what the work did not. The editor, the conductor, the director, the dramaturg, the producer — the figures whose role inside a creative tradition is to attend to the whole on the maker's behalf — exist precisely because the discipline of subordination is structurally hard for the person inside the part.

What the maker can do, working alone, is approximated through a small number of practical moves.

The first is to read the work at the level of the work, not the level of the part. The chapter is to be read as a chapter, not as a sequence of sentences; the symphony is to be heard as a symphony, not as a sequence of phrases. The maker who can hold the higher level for the duration of a reading or a hearing has, in that act, performed the assessment the work requires. The sentence that was beautiful on the line will, in the chapter-level reading, either continue to function or cease to function. The maker's job is to notice which.

The second is to write past the part the maker is in love with. Continuing to write — drafting the next chapter, the next movement, the next scene — almost always reveals what the loved part is doing to the surrounding work. The work as it accumulates becomes the standard against which the loved part is judged; the work, in becoming itself, demonstrates whether the part fits. The maker who refuses to continue writing until the loved part is settled has, by that refusal, made the part the standard the surrounding work has to meet. This is the wrong direction.

The third is to allow time to do its work. The part the maker cannot cut today will, six months from now, look different. Some loved parts survive the six months and reveal themselves to be load-bearing. Most do not. The maker who has the patience to set the work aside, return to it, and read it as if it were not the maker's own is performing, on the work, the assessment the work would otherwise have to receive from an editor. The cold reading is not a substitute for an editor; it is a substitute for the maker's attachment to the moment of making, which is what the cold reading sets aside.

The fourth is to remember what the work is for. The work is not for the part. The work is for the reader, the listener, the viewer, the audience that will receive it. Tolstoy's structural claim survives whatever one thinks of his moralism: the work is the transmission, not the artefact. A part that interferes with the transmission has failed at being a part of this work, however successful it has been at being itself.

## Closing

Most of what has been said about the maker's dilemma in the literature of craft is, on this analysis, the literature of the systems tradition under a different vocabulary. The discipline of subordination, named by Ackoff in his lectures on design, is the discipline every working maker has been struggling toward inside the work. The recognition that the part that performs best in isolation is rarely the part that performs best in combination, named by the systems literature as a structural feature of any complicated whole, is the recognition every editor has tried to communicate to every writer who could not yet hear it.[^9]

What the maker is doing, when the discipline holds, is what the systems tradition recommends in every other domain it has touched: making the parts as well as they can be made, and then submitting the parts to the test of the whole, and accepting the whole's verdict on what the parts can be allowed to do. The verdict is sometimes harsh. Sentences are cut; movements are rewritten; scenes are removed. The work that survives the verdict is the work that has been made for the reader and not for the maker's love of what the maker has done.

This is not a counsel of austerity. The work that emerges from this discipline is more, not less, than the work that does not. The reader receives, from a work that has been so disciplined, a continuous attention rather than a sequence of admirations; what is received is the work itself, which is more than any of its parts and which would not have been available had the parts been allowed to dominate. The maker has, in subordinating the parts, made something that the parts together could not have made.

The discipline is the most concrete instance of a principle that runs through the systems tradition: the whole is not the sum of the parts. The whole is what the parts can produce, in the right configuration, when each is performing its function in service of the rest. The maker's work is to find that configuration. The dilemma is that finding it requires giving up, repeatedly, the parts the maker has loved. The reward, when the configuration is found, is the work itself.
