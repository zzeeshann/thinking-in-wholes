# Chapter 2 — The Three Returns

The argument of the previous chapter was that the modern world's working vocabulary was built around a method whose strength is the analysis of separable parts, and whose weakness is the analysis of wholes. This chapter is about three figures who, in the second half of the twentieth century, set out to repair that weakness — not by replacing analysis but by adding to it the missing operation of synthesis. Each came at the work from a different direction. Each returned to the working vocabulary something it had quietly let go. None of them, taken alone, would be enough; together they form the recovery the book draws on.

The three are Russell Ackoff, Donella Meadows, and Peter Senge. Ackoff was born in 1919 and trained as a philosopher; he spent his working life inside the corporate management he was trying to repair. Meadows was born in 1941 and trained as a biophysicist; she spent her working life modelling the dynamics of large-scale systems and writing for a public audience that would take the modelling seriously. Senge was born in 1947 and trained as an engineer; he spent his working life inside the practice of teaching organisations how to learn. Their lifespans overlap; their friendships and conversations are documented in their books and the work of their students; the disagreements between them are real and worth taking seriously. The temptation, in any retrospective treatment, is to flatten the three into a single doctrine. The discipline is to keep them three.

What each returned to the working vocabulary is the subject of this chapter. Ackoff returned purpose. Meadows returned dynamics. Senge returned practice. Each is partial. Each is necessary. Each had to do its work against the gravitational pull of the analytic culture the previous chapter described, and each did so by drawing on a tradition that had not died but had become invisible — Singer and Churchman in Ackoff's case, Forrester in Meadows' and Senge's, the older cybernetics of Wiener and Beer in the background of all three.

Ackoff is the right place to begin, because his return is the one that was hardest to make in the corporate setting where he worked. By the middle of the twentieth century, the operations-research tradition Ackoff had helped to build in the 1950s had matured into a quantitative discipline for finding the most efficient way to do whatever a corporation had decided to do. The discipline answered the wrong question. It assumed that a company's purposes were given, that the only remaining work was the optimisation of means against those purposes, and that the better the optimisation the better the result. Ackoff watched, across a forty-year career, what this assumption produced: companies that hit every quarterly target on the way to becoming uninhabitable for their employees, indistinguishable from their competitors, and structurally unable to adapt to a world that had moved on from the purpose they had been built around.

What Ackoff returned was the prior question. Before asking how a system should be optimised, he insisted, one had to ask what the system was for; and that question, properly asked, almost never had the answer one expected. Ackoff observed, across thousands of consulting engagements, that the stated purpose of a corporation — to maximise shareholder value, to grow market share, to dominate a category — was almost always a substitute for a purpose the corporation had failed to articulate. The substitution made the work measurable. It also made the work pointless. A corporation that had organised itself around the maximisation of a number was, in his diagnosis, a corporation that had given up the harder work of deciding what it would have wanted to be remembered for.[^1]

The diagnostic was matched by a method. Ackoff called it idealised design. The method asked the people inside an organisation to imagine that the organisation had been destroyed overnight, and that they had the chance to design its replacement from scratch — with two constraints: the replacement had to be technologically feasible (no magic), and it had to be operationally viable (it had to work tomorrow). Within those constraints, anything was permitted. Existing arrangements, sunk costs, accumulated job titles, departmental loyalties — all were available to be discarded if the design did not require them. The exercise sounded utopian; in Ackoff's hands it was not. It was a method for surfacing the disconnect between an organisation's current arrangements and its actual purposes, and for producing a design the people inside it could agree to work toward. He distinguished between problem-solving (finding the cause of trouble), problem-resolution (treating the symptom), and problem-dissolution (redesigning the system so the problem could no longer arise). Idealised design was the method of dissolution. It was the only one of the three that the analytic tradition had no equivalent for.

What Ackoff got wrong was his estimate of how readily corporate institutions would accept the diagnosis. He believed, through most of his working life, that the discipline of asking what a corporation was for would, once introduced, prove its own value and spread on its merits. It did not. The institutions he worked with were structurally incapable of holding the question for very long. Their incentive structures, their reporting hierarchies, their quarterly cycles, the way their senior officers were trained and rewarded — all conspired to push the corporation back, after Ackoff left the room, to the more measurable but less coherent state it had been in before. Ackoff saw this. He did not fully credit how comprehensive the resistance was, and his late writings have a slight tone of injured surprise that the corporate world had not, by the early twenty-first century, organised itself around purposes more developed than the ones he had spent fifty years arguing against. The diagnosis was right. The estimate of receptivity was not.

If Ackoff returned purpose, Donella Meadows returned dynamics. Meadows trained as a biophysicist, took a doctorate at Harvard, joined the Dartmouth faculty, and spent the central part of her career applying the system dynamics method developed by Jay Forrester to a series of large-scale problems whose behaviour the analytic tradition could not explain. The Limits to Growth study of 1972, on which she was a principal author, was an early and widely-discussed application of the method to the question of long-term human resource use. Her later writings — published essays through the 1980s and 1990s, the standalone *Leverage Points* paper of 1999, and the manuscript that became *Thinking in Systems* — set out the working vocabulary that has since become the lingua franca of the field.[^2]

What Meadows returned was the recognition that systems behave according to their structure, not their parts. A reinforcing loop produces growth and collapse regardless of the particular substances flowing through it; a balancing loop produces equilibrium regardless of the particular variables it controls; a delay between an action and its consequence produces overshoot and oscillation regardless of the field in which the delay appears. The modern world had spent three centuries describing the parts of its systems — the chemicals, the species, the financial instruments, the labour categories — and very little time describing the arrangements of those parts. Meadows insisted, repeatedly and with great patience, that the arrangement was the thing. A system in which a stock is regulated by a balancing loop with a long delay will overshoot its equilibrium whether the stock is a population, a bank account, or the carbon dioxide concentration of the atmosphere. The structure was what one had to see. Once seen, it could be reasoned about.

The most consequential of her later contributions was the *Leverage Points* essay, in which she ranked twelve places to intervene in a system in ascending order of leverage.[^3] Adjusting parameters — taxes, subsidies, standards — was, on her account, the lowest-leverage intervention available to a policy-maker. Changing the goals of the system was higher. Changing the paradigm out of which the goals were drawn was higher still. Transcending paradigms — the rare moment when a culture recognises that its operating worldview is one possible worldview among many — was the highest of all, and the rarest. The list is now widely cited; it is also widely misread. Meadows did not mean that policy-makers should always reach for the highest-leverage intervention. She meant that the leverage of an intervention is not what policy-makers usually optimise for, and that recognising what they are giving up by intervening at low leverage is itself a form of seeing.

Meadows is the most ethically demanding of the three. She wrote repeatedly that the modeller of a system has obligations to the system being modelled — obligations of humility, of honesty about uncertainty, of responsibility for what the model is used to do. The systems thinker who could see the structure had a duty not to use the seeing for narrow advantage, because the structure described other people's lives. This is a position more ethical than methodological, and it is the one she articulated more clearly than either Ackoff or Senge.

What Meadows got wrong, if "wrong" is the right word for the limits of an honest position, was the political bottleneck. She believed that systemic insight, made widely available, would produce systemic action. The first half of the belief proved more achievable than the second. *Thinking in Systems* and *Leverage Points* did, in fact, become widely available; the vocabulary of stocks, flows, feedback, and leverage entered policy schools, training programmes, and a generation of public discussion. The action did not follow at the rate the insight would have predicted. The political institutions that would have to act on systemic insight turn out to be themselves systems, with their own structures and their own balancing loops, and those structures resist systemic action in a way Meadows underestimated. She was not wrong about what should be done. She was wrong about what could be done by argument alone.

Peter Senge returned practice. Senge studied at MIT under Forrester and took his doctorate in management; his contribution was to translate the system dynamics tradition into a discipline that an organisation could adopt and live by. *The Fifth Discipline*, published in 1990, is the central text.[^4] The argument of the book is that an organisation, like a person, can learn — but only if its members cultivate a set of practices that the analytic culture had let atrophy. Senge named five: personal mastery (the discipline of being clear about what one is trying to bring about), mental models (the discipline of examining one's own assumptions), shared vision (the discipline of building a picture of the future an organisation will work toward), team learning (the discipline of inquiry and reflection in groups), and systems thinking (the discipline that holds the others together). The first four had been practised, in some form, by craftsmen, families, religious orders, and small enterprises throughout history. The fifth — systems thinking — was the new contribution, and the one for which the book was titled.

Senge's claim was that the five practices reinforce each other. Personal mastery without systems thinking produces individuals who optimise their own goals against a structure they have not learned to see. Systems thinking without team learning produces analysts who can see the structure but cannot act on it together. Shared vision without mental-model work produces organisations that commit to futures their members have not actually chosen. The five disciplines were, on Senge's account, the operating practices of an organisation that took its own learning seriously — and the conditions under which the systems-thinking insight could become an organisational rather than an individual achievement. The *Fieldbook*, published with Kleiner, Roberts, Ross, and Smith in 1994, supplied the practical tools the original book had described in principle.[^5]

What Senge got wrong was nearer to him than to Ackoff or Meadows, and harder for him to see clearly while it was happening. The framework was widely adopted in form. Many corporations announced themselves "learning organisations." Most did not change the structures that were preventing their members from learning. The five disciplines were taken up as a vocabulary — a set of slides, a set of training modules, a set of officers whose titles included the word "learning" — and the underlying systems remained, in many cases, exactly the systems Senge had described as obstacles to learning. Senge himself acknowledged this in the 2006 revised edition of the book, which is more sober about the diffusion record than the 1990 first edition could be. The diagnosis is that the practice cannot be installed; it has to be cultivated, and cultivation is slower than installation, and most institutions are not patient. The framework worked where it was adopted in substance. It worked less well where it was adopted in form.

The three were not in lockstep. Their disagreements, where they were explicit, were as instructive as their points of agreement, and they cluster around three questions.

The first question is the scale at which the systems response should run. Ackoff worked at the corporate level — he assumed that the organisation was the natural unit of intervention and that the design of the corporation was where the work had to be done. Meadows worked at the planetary level — she assumed that the natural unit was the biosphere, the resource base, the global economy, and that no smaller intervention could meet the scale of what was needed. Senge worked at the level of the team and the organisation — he assumed that the practice of learning had to be cultivated in groups small enough to know each other, and that no top-down design or planetary model would produce the kind of capacity that the period required. Each held the others' positions with respect, but each thought their own scale was the load-bearing one. The book's view, developed across the chapters that follow, is that all three are right at the scales they worked at, and that the systems response runs on persons capable of moving between scales without losing sight of any.

The second question is the role of formal modelling. Meadows, building on Forrester's method, treated computer simulation as a central technical contribution of the systems tradition — the means by which complexity could be reasoned about with precision. Ackoff was more skeptical. He thought computer models, however well-built, captured only the variables their builders had thought to include, and that the variables that mattered most were often the ones the model could not represent. He preferred the qualitative discipline of idealised design, in which the people inside an organisation worked out the variables for themselves. Senge sat between the two: his framework drew on Forrester's archetypes of system behaviour, but he treated them as conceptual tools for organisational conversation rather than as quantitative models to be solved. The disagreement is worth taking seriously. It is the question of how much the systems response should depend on technical apparatus, and how much on the cultivated judgement of the people inside the system. The position the book takes is that the answer changes with the scale: at the planetary scale, where time horizons are long and parts of the system are not directly observable, modelling earns its keep; at the organisational and personal scales, where the variables that matter are mostly social, qualitative discipline is the more honest tool.

The third question is whether institutions can be reformed from within. Senge's project rests on the answer being yes — that an organisation can, with enough discipline, become a learning organisation capable of seeing and changing its own structure. Ackoff's late writings answer cautiously yes: idealised design is, after all, a method for the inside, requiring the people who will live with the result to do the design themselves. Meadows is the most pessimistic of the three. She came to think that the deeper structures — the global economy's commitment to growth, the political institutions' commitment to short cycles, the cultural commitment to consumption — could not be reformed from within their own logics, and that the changes required would arrive either by external pressure or by collapse. None of the three positions is, in retrospect, simply right or simply wrong. Meadows was right about the deepest structures, Senge and Ackoff were right about more local ones, and the question of which scale one is working at determines which of the three diagnoses applies.

The three returns are not the whole of the systems tradition. They are the most accessible part of it — the part most likely to be read by a manager, a designer, or a public-policy student, and the part most likely to shape the working vocabulary of the next generation. Behind them stand four figures whose contributions are quieter but whose absence would leave the tradition incomplete.

Jay Forrester is the methodological ground. *Industrial Dynamics*, published in 1961, established system dynamics as a working discipline — a way of building computer models of management situations that could simulate the behaviour of stocks, flows, and feedback loops over time.[^6] Forrester had come to MIT from wartime work on naval fire-control systems and the early development of digital computing; his contribution to systems thinking was the marriage of the older cybernetic insights to a tractable computational method. Both Meadows and Senge are, in important respects, Forrester's intellectual descendants. The Limits to Growth model that made Meadows publicly visible was an application of the system dynamics method she had learned from him. *The Fifth Discipline* opens with archetypes of system behaviour that Senge first encountered in Forrester's classes.

C. West Churchman supplied the ethics. *The Systems Approach*, published in 1968, set out the position that any intervention in a system is a moral act — that the systems thinker has commitments to the people inside the system being studied that the analytic tradition does not have a place for.[^7] Churchman was the philosophical bridge between the Singer tradition at Pennsylvania and the management-science generation that came after; he edited Singer's posthumous *Experience and Reflection* in 1959 and carried Singer's experimentalist philosophy into Berkeley, where he taught for the second half of his career. The ethical seriousness one finds in Meadows owes more to Churchman than is usually credited.

Stafford Beer supplied the cybernetics. *Brain of the Firm*, first published in London in 1972, set out the Viable System Model — a structural account of what an organisation needs in order to remain coherent under change.[^8] Beer's argument was that an organisation's information flows have to be designed deliberately, on cybernetic principles, or the organisation will become incoherent and fail to respond to its environment in time. The most consequential application of the model was Beer's work for the government of Salvador Allende in Chile in the early 1970s — Project Cybersyn, an attempt to build a real-time information system for the management of the nationalised economy. The project ended with the 1973 coup; its archive is now a standard reference in the history of cybernetics. The model survived the project. It is the most fully developed account of organisational structure the tradition has produced.

Jamshid Gharajedaghi supplied the practice of design. *Systems Thinking: Managing Chaos and Complexity* — third edition published in 2011 — distils four decades of work alongside Ackoff at Wharton and in the consulting practice they ran together.[^9] Gharajedaghi's contribution was the iterative design method that took Ackoff's idealised design out of the consulting engagement and into a workable practice for designing the architecture of organisations from the whole down. The book is the most complete practitioner's manual the tradition has. It is also the least known of the four.

The four supplements stand to the three returns roughly as a chassis stands to the engine that drives it. Forrester is the method; Churchman is the ethics; Beer is the structure; Gharajedaghi is the design practice. Without them the three returns would float. With them, what Ackoff and Meadows and Senge offered becomes a working tradition — one that can be taught, practised, and extended by people who did not know any of the original figures personally.

This is the tradition the rest of the book is in conversation with. Each of the chapters that follow draws on it — on Ackoff for the question of purpose, on Meadows for the discipline of structure, on Senge for the practice of learning, on Forrester for the method of modelling, on Churchman for the ethics of intervention, on Beer for the architecture of viable institutions, on Gharajedaghi for the practice of design from the whole. The tradition is rich. The reason it has not, so far, become the dominant intellectual culture of the modern world has less to do with the quality of the work than with the structural resistances the work was always going to meet. The chapters of Part Two, which follow, name those resistances directly.

What the three returns share, beneath their differences, is a recognition that the analytic culture had stopped seeing what it could not measure. Purpose cannot be measured against a benchmark; dynamics cannot be measured at a single point in time; practice cannot be measured by the publication of a manual. The three returns are returns to questions the analytic culture had quietly stopped asking, because asking them carried the risk of producing answers that could not be reduced to a number. None of the three abandoned measurement. Each insisted on it where it could be done honestly. What each refused to do was substitute the measurement for the question. That refusal, repeated across the seven figures of the tradition, is the discipline the rest of the book is built on.
