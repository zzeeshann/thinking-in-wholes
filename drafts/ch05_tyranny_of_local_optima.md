# Chapter 5 — The Tyranny of Local Optima

Russell Ackoff used to ask his audiences to imagine the following project. An engineer is given the task of building the best automobile in the world. He has resources, time, and a clear method. He acquires one of every production car currently on the market — a hundred and forty-two of them, in the version of the story Ackoff most often told — and he submits each car to careful examination. He notes which car has the best engine. Which has the best transmission. Which has the best brakes, the best steering column, the best suspension, the best body, the best electrical system. When the survey is complete, he has, in effect, the world's best automobile in pieces, distributed across one hundred and forty-two vehicles. He has only to assemble it. So he does. He removes the best engine from the car that has it, and the best transmission from the car that has that, and so on through the inventory, and he carries the parts to a workshop, where he assembles them into a single automobile. The result does not run.[^1]

The result does not run because the engine he has chosen was designed for a different transmission, and the transmission for a different chassis, and the chassis to carry a different weight, and the brakes were calibrated for a different mass and a different velocity profile, and the electrical system was wired for a different draw. Each part is, by the criterion under which it was selected, the best of its kind. Together they cannot make a car. The best engine in the world is the best engine in the world only inside the car for which it was designed; outside that car, it is a piece of metal of no particular value to anyone. The performance of the whole depends not on how each part performs in isolation but on how the parts perform in combination — on the fit, on the sequencing, on the mutual adjustment of one part to the next.

This is the lesson of the chapter, and it is older and harder than it looks. The sentence sounds like common sense. It is. But the practices that contradict it govern most of the institutions a person will encounter in a long life — hospitals, schools, factories, militaries, corporations, ministries — and very nearly every method by which those institutions try to improve themselves. To say that a system is more than the sum of its parts is to say something that is rarely contested in the abstract and almost never honoured in practice. The reason is not stupidity; the reason is that the practice of optimising each part separately is enormously easier than the practice of designing wholes. Easier to teach, easier to manage, easier to measure. The price is that the wholes do not work.

## The pattern outside the factory

Strip the example of its automotive setting and the same shape appears wherever human beings try to improve a complex system by improving its parts.

Consider a patient with five specialists. Her cardiologist optimises her heart against the cardiology textbooks. Her endocrinologist optimises her thyroid function against the endocrinology textbooks. Her gastroenterologist treats her ulcer. Her rheumatologist addresses her joint pain. Her psychiatrist manages her depression. Each specialist is competent within the territory of his discipline, and each medication is, by the standard of its discipline, the right medication. The patient gets worse. She gets worse because her body is not five organs but one organism, because the medication that is optimal for her thyroid interacts with the medication that is optimal for her heart, because the depression is in part the body's response to the joint pain, and because no one has been asked, and no one has the standing to ask, what is happening to the patient as a whole. The general practitioner who used to hold the whole picture has been replaced by a referral system; the referral system optimises specialty access; the patient is the residue. Medicine practised this way is medicine that treats organs rather than people. It is not bad medicine in the technical sense — within each specialty the standard of care may be high — but it is medicine that fails the patient because no one is treating the patient.[^2]

Or consider the school. A school decides to improve, and improvement is operationalised as test scores: in mathematics, in reading, in the language of instruction, in some places in science. Each subject is taught in isolation, by a teacher trained in that subject, against a curriculum prepared for that subject, and assessed by an examination written for that subject. The school improves on each measure, and each year the head reports gains. What does the graduate look like? The graduate is fluent in mathematics for an hour at a time, and in reading for an hour at a time, and in science for an hour at a time, but no one has asked the graduate to do anything that requires the three at once, because the school did not contain a setting in which that question could be put. The graduate is, in the precise sense the engineer above produced — assembled from the best parts of unrelated cars. The school has improved each subject by treating each subject as if its purpose were to be itself. The purpose of a school, on any honest accounting, is the formation of a person who can act in a world that does not present its problems by subject. That purpose has no department.

Or consider the city. The road system is optimised for vehicle throughput. The building code is optimised for energy efficiency. The school district is optimised for test scores. The hospital is optimised for procedural volume. The park system is optimised for maintenance cost per acre. Each optimisation is led by a competent professional with appropriate credentials, and each is carried out against a set of measurements that have been worked over for decades. The roads grow wider; the buildings grow tighter; the schools grow more measurable; the hospitals grow more procedural; the parks grow plainer. The city in which a person lives — in which a person walks to the corner, sees a friend, raises a child, ages, dies — is not the sum of these optimisations. The city is what one inhabits when one is not the road system or the building code. The city declines as each part improves, and the people who live there cannot say why; they only say it does not feel as it once felt. They are right. The whole has been disassembled into parts, each well-tended, and the connective tissue that made the whole inhabitable has been quietly cut.

The pattern is not a business pattern. It is a pattern of mistaking the part for the unit of analysis. The factory was where the pattern was first formalised — Frederick Taylor's stopwatch, the assembly line, the great twentieth-century theorem that the way to improve a process was to break it into the smallest measurable steps and improve each — but the pattern does not belong to the factory. The factory borrowed it from a much older intellectual habit, the habit of analysis: take the thing apart, study each piece, then act as if the sum of the pieces is the thing. The factory generalised the habit and lent it the prestige of nineteenth-century engineering, which the rest of the institutional world borrowed back, gladly. By the late twentieth century the practice was universal. By the early twenty-first it was beginning to fail visibly enough that the failure could no longer be attributed to insufficient zeal in carrying it out.

## What measurement does to what it measures

A separate and related failure has its own laws, and the chapter must name them, because the misuse of measurement is the form in which the tyranny of local optima usually arrives. The two laws were stated independently, by two researchers in two different fields, within a few years of each other in the late 1970s, looking at two different problems. They saw the same thing.

Charles Goodhart was an economist at the Bank of England in 1975 when the Reserve Bank of Australia held a conference on monetary economics in Sydney. He gave a paper on the British monetary management of the previous decade. The paper recorded a pattern the Bank of England had observed. The British authorities had identified certain monetary aggregates — particular measurable quantities of money, narrowly or broadly defined — that, in their historical data, behaved in stable, predictable, useful relationships with the things the Bank cared about: inflation, output, unemployment. So the authorities began to treat those aggregates as targets, in the technical sense — quantities to be controlled. As soon as they did, the relationships dissolved. The aggregates kept being measurable, but their behaviour stopped being a useful indicator of anything in particular. In the footnote of the paper Goodhart formalised what they had seen: any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes. The regularity holds while the measure is an indicator. It dissolves the moment the measure becomes a target.[^3]

Donald Campbell was a social psychologist working in the methodology of program evaluation. In 1979, in *Evaluation and Program Planning*, he stated the same observation in a more general form, drawn from a wider class of cases: the more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures, and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor.[^4] He had noticed it everywhere — in standardised testing, where the test, once high-stakes, ceases to measure what it was designed to measure; in policing, where crime statistics, once tied to promotion and budget, cease to measure crime; in hospital reporting, where mortality figures, once made consequential, cease to measure mortality. The pattern is the same as Goodhart's, generalised. Wherever a measure is held lightly — used to inform, to indicate, to suggest — it can carry the weight of an indicator. Wherever a measure is held tightly — used to control, to reward, to punish, to allocate — it ceases to be an indicator and becomes a thing in itself, a quantity that the system around it learns to produce.

The two laws together name a structural property of feedback in human systems. They do not say that measurement is corrupting, or that quantification is bad, or that data should be distrusted. They say something narrower and more useful. They say that any measure used as a target is, by that act, severed from the thing it was originally a measure of. The patient's blood-pressure number, once the doctor's career hangs on it, is no longer the patient's blood pressure; it is the number on a chart, which can be optimised by a hundred means that have nothing to do with the patient's circulation. The school's test scores, once the school's funding hangs on them, are no longer a measure of what students have learned; they are a measure of how the school has organised itself around the test. The corporation's quarterly earnings, once the corporation's executives are paid on them, are no longer a measure of the corporation's health; they are a measure of how the executives have arranged the books. None of this is fraud, except in the limit. It is the ordinary intelligent response of a human system to a feedback loop that has been pointed at one of its measurements.

A book published in 2018 collected the case studies in their adult form: medicine, policing, education, the military, the universities, philanthropy, business.[^5] The cases differed in their domains and were almost interchangeable in their structure. In each case a measure had been chosen, often shrewdly; in each case the measure had been made consequential; in each case the system had reorganised itself to produce the measure rather than the thing the measure had once tracked. In each case the productivity of the measurement effort had been impressive, the productivity of the measured activity had declined, and the people who actually did the work had quietly lost faith in the whole apparatus. The book noted, mildly, that this had been predictable since at least Campbell's 1979 essay, and Goodhart's 1975 paper before that, and the older arguments behind both. The book was correct.

## Why the local optimum is so hard to give up

Why do institutions persist in this practice when the failure is so well documented? Three reasons, each respectable on its own terms, and dangerous in combination.

The first is that the local optimum is measurable and the global optimum is not, or not without much more difficulty. A hospital can measure procedure-completion time. It cannot, in any equivalent sense, measure whether the patient lived a better life because of what was done. A school can measure a test score; it cannot, on any timescale shorter than a generation, measure whether the graduate flourished. A road authority can measure throughput per lane-hour; it cannot measure whether the city is one in which it is good to grow old. The temptation is not to lie about what is measurable; the temptation is to forget that what is measurable is not the same as what matters. The forgetting accumulates. By the second decade of an institution's life, the forgetting is the institution.

The second is that the local optimum can be assigned to an individual, and the global optimum cannot. A surgeon is responsible for the surgery; a teacher for the test scores; a marketing director for the marketing numbers. Each can be praised, paid, blamed, or fired on the basis of his discipline's measurements. There is no one who is responsible for the patient's life-as-a-whole, the student's formation-as-a-person, the customer's experience-of-being-a-customer-over-twenty-years. To create such a role would be to create a role that cannot be evaluated within a single decade, and modern institutions do not know what to do with such roles, because they have built their personnel systems around shorter feedback loops. The result is that nobody is responsible for the wholes that the institutions exist to serve. The wholes degrade, and the personnel system, asked who is at fault, finds that everyone has met his metrics.

The third reason is that the language of local optimisation is the language of professional competence. To improve one's part of a system requires technical mastery, training, certification, conferences, journals, and a community of peers who recognise good work in that part. To attend to the whole requires none of these, exactly, and is for that reason often suspected of being amateur. The systems tradition has lived for seventy years with the accusation that it is a form of generalist hand-waving — that the people who think about wholes are people who could not master parts. The accusation is wrong, but it has the durability of a professional anxiety, and it explains why the practice of attending to wholes has remained, for all the literature, a marginal one. Specialisation is institutionally rewarded; integration is not. A career is built by going deeper into one part. Going across the parts is, by the standards of every promotion committee, a waste of effort.

These three reasons are not arguments for the practice of local optimisation. They are explanations of why it persists, even where its costs are visible. Naming them is necessary, because the alternative — pretending that the practice is irrational, or that its proponents are merely careless — leads to the kind of advice that does not survive contact with an organisation. The systems tradition has been giving better advice than it has been able to make stick, for a long time, partly because it has not always faced what it was up against.

## What the tradition recommends instead

Three commitments, each demanding, each better than the alternative.

The first is to subordinate measurement to purpose, rather than the other way around. Begin with the question of what the whole is for. Then ask what behaviour, in the parts, would constitute that purpose being served. Then, only then, ask what could be measured that would indicate, with reasonable accuracy, whether such behaviour was happening. Hold the measure loosely. The moment it ceases to indicate the purpose — because the parts have learned to produce the measure without producing the purpose — discard it and find another. Treat any single measure with the suspicion the Goodhart and Campbell laws warrant: as a useful temporary indicator, never as a target to be controlled.[^6]

The second is to design at the level of the whole and not at the level of the parts. The systems tradition's term for this discipline is *idealised design*, and Ackoff was its most rigorous proponent: the designer's task is to specify the system that the participants would, if free to start over, produce, and then to work backwards from that specification to the changes that the present arrangements admit.[^7] The specification governs the parts. The parts are then chosen — and modified, and abandoned where necessary — for their fit to the specification, not for their performance against intra-part measures. The discipline is uncomfortable because it asks the engineer to leave each part less than its possible best in order to make the whole work. This was the engineer's mistake with the 142 cars. He did not know to leave the second-best engine where it was, because the second-best engine was the one that fit the chassis he had chosen. He had no chassis he had chosen. He had only a list of bests, drawn from systems he was not building.

The third is to recognise, plainly, that the part that performs best in isolation is rarely the part that performs best in combination, and to act on the recognition. The athlete who is the best at his position is not always the player who makes the team better; sometimes the team's best player is the one who plays a less spectacular role and frees the others. The employee who hits every individual target is not always the colleague who makes the department work; sometimes the department's most useful person is the one who carries the work that does not appear in anyone's metrics. The component that scores highest on the bench-test is not always the component that integrates well with the rest of the assembly; sometimes the right component is the one whose specifications are unimpressive on their own and whose virtue is its compatibility with the next component over. To name this is to name the modesty that the systems tradition has been recommending, and the world has been resisting, for sixty years. It is also, in any honest accounting of how things actually work, the truth.

The chapter has been about the cost of forgetting that wholes are not assembled from optimised parts. The cost is paid by the patient who got worse on five excellent medications, the graduate who is fluent in three subjects and useless in the fourth where the three were supposed to combine, the city whose every system improved while the city itself declined, the corporation whose every department met its targets in the year the corporation lost its way. The cost is paid wherever a measurement, sincerely chosen, has become a thing produced for its own sake, and the parts have been improved against it while the whole has gone on quietly degrading.

The next two chapters take the analysis further. The pattern of local optimisation persists in part because we cannot see the loops we are inside; that is the next chapter. And it persists in part because we are wrong about time — wrong about how long it takes for an action to produce its effect, wrong about which delays are reasonable to accept, wrong about how much patience the better practice requires of us. That is the chapter after.

For now the lesson holds: the parts, optimised separately, do not assemble into a working whole. They never have. The reason is not in any one part but in the relations among them, which is to say in the system, which is to say in what the systems tradition has been pointing at the whole time, and what the rest of this book is for.
