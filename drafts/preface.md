# Preface

*Letter to the 2040 Reader*

A book written for a future reader is a strange object. The writer cannot know what the reader knows, which means every confident sentence carries a small risk that only the reader can see. By the time these words are read, much that now seems urgent will have fallen out of memory, and much that now seems unlikely will have settled into the unremarkable furniture of an ordinary life. The writer offers what he can see. The reader, fourteen years on, looks back across the gap and decides what was true.

This book is addressed to a reader in the year 2040. Not because the writer believes himself capable of seeing that far — no one is — but because the discipline of writing for that reader is the only one that produces honest prose at this moment. The temptation in 2026 is to write for 2026: to name the controversy that everyone is naming, to denounce the figure that everyone is denouncing, to use the vocabulary that signals one belongs to the right group. None of this prose survives a decade. The 2040 reader will have moved past these names, these arguments, the small wars over phrases that consumed so much attention here. They will read the prose of this moment the way one reads an old newspaper found in a drawer: with curiosity about what the writers were so sure of, and what they failed to notice.

So the book pulls itself out of its own decade. It writes around the moment rather than from inside it. The cost is some loss of urgency. The compensation is the chance — small but real — that some of what is here will still be useful when the moment has passed.

What the book is about is not new. The thinkers it draws on did most of their work in the second half of the twentieth century, and stood on a tradition older than that. Russell Ackoff worked from the late 1940s into the early 2000s, mostly at the Wharton School, building a systems view of organisations that almost nobody in business actually used.[^1] Donella Meadows wrote her major book in the early 1990s; it sat in draft form for years and was published only after her death.[^2] Peter Senge took the work into the corporate world with *The Fifth Discipline*, and watched it become, in many places, a vocabulary used to dress up the same parts-thinking the book was written to oppose. Edgar Singer, the philosopher whose lineage runs through Ackoff and Churchman, was making the relevant arguments in the 1920s and 1930s.[^3] The systems vocabulary itself is older still: Whitehead's Lowell Lectures were given in 1925; Bergson's *Creative Evolution* appeared in French in 1907.[^4]

This is not, in any sense that matters, new thinking. It is old thinking that the working world has not yet absorbed.

Why has it taken so long? The honest answer is that parts-thinking has been astonishingly successful. For three and a half centuries, breaking the world into pieces and analysing the pieces in isolation produced a standard of life and a standard of knowledge that no previous civilisation came near. The clock was understood. The disease was treated. The factory ran. The bridge stood. The argument that the systems thinkers have made for half a century — that this method has limits, and that the limits matter — has been heard, mostly, as an attack on a method that seems obviously to work. Why fix what is producing such clear results?

The reason this book is being written now, and not in 1971 or 1991, is that the limits are no longer a matter of argument. They are a matter of observation. The patterns the systems thinkers warned about — the institutions that optimise themselves into incoherence, the treatments that worsen the disease, the economies that grow themselves out of habitability — are no longer warnings. They are the news. By 2040 they will be the history. The reader of that year will not need to be persuaded that parts-thinking has failed at the scales that matter most. They will need to know what was being thought, in those years, by the people who were trying to think otherwise.

That is what this book is: a record of what could be seen, from inside one of those years, of a way of thinking the world had not yet learned to use.

The writer is acutely aware of how little he can see clearly from where he stands. He can recognise the patterns that the older systems thinkers named, and find them in the world around him. He cannot see, with any reliability, what those patterns will produce next. He is in the position of a person reading a long novel who has reached the middle: he understands the shape, the recurring themes, the kinds of trouble the characters keep finding themselves in. He cannot tell how the book ends. Anyone who claims, in 2026, to know how the next decade ends is selling something.

So the book does not predict in detail. It names the patterns. It engages the thinkers who saw them most clearly. It tries to give the reader of 2040 a sense of the intellectual ground — the unread or half-read tradition — on which a different way of working might be built. Where a prediction is made, it goes on the record, with a date, and the 2040 reader has every right to grade it. There is no other way to write honestly about the future.

There are things the writer knows he cannot see clearly. They are worth naming.

He cannot see what artificial intelligence — taken as a class of technology, not as the products of any particular company — will turn out to mean for human work, human attention, and human meaning over the coming decade. He has hypotheses. He suspects the technology is poorly named: that it is neither artificial in the sense of being separable from its makers, nor intelligent in the sense of being whole. But he does not know what it will do to the world it has entered. Anyone who does is wrong.

The reorganisation of the climate is one of the things he can see is happening but cannot see the consequences of. He can see that institutions designed for a stable climate cannot operate inside an unstable one. He cannot see which institutions will adapt, which will collapse, and which will be replaced by something the present has no name for.

He cannot see what the breakdown of the post-war international order will produce. He can see it breaking. He can see that assumptions on which several generations were educated — about peace, about trade, about the predictability of the weather — have ceased to apply. He cannot see what comes after.

He suspects that the most important developments of the coming fifteen years will not be the ones currently being announced. The dominant news of any decade is rarely the dominant fact of it. The 1970s spent its time arguing about the wrong things; the 1990s did the same; the 2010s did the same. There is no reason to expect the present decade to be the exception. What this book can do is point at the older intellectual tradition that has, in most decades, turned out to have been quietly more important than the news suggested. Whether that turns out to be true of this decade as well, the 2040 reader is in a better position to know than the writer.

The book draws on a small number of thinkers, treated as primary texts rather than as figures cited in passing. Ackoff and Meadows are the most heavily used, because they wrote the most accessible serious work. Singer is engaged because his concept of producer-product causation is the most useful philosophical anchor for what the book is trying to do, and because he is the most badly underread member of the tradition. Christopher Alexander appears in the chapter on architecture, because no one has written more carefully about the difference between a part-by-part design and one that holds the whole.[^5] Jane Jacobs appears in the same chapter, because she was the rare person who could see a city as a living system without making it sentimental.[^6] Whitehead and Bergson appear in the chapter on philosophy, because the systems turn in the working world has barely begun to register the older systems turn in philosophy, and there is overdue work to be done in connecting them.

Each of these thinkers is read in their own words, not in summaries. The author has tried, where possible, to argue with them rather than simply transmit them. They were wrong about some things. Ackoff overestimated how quickly the corporate world would absorb what he was saying. Meadows underestimated how hard it would be for political systems to act on the kind of analysis she did. Senge's framework, taught at scale, became in many places a vocabulary for the very pathologies it was meant to address. Singer was not always clear. Alexander, late in his life, made some claims about beauty the reader has to take on faith or not. Naming these limits is part of taking the work seriously. A canon that cannot be argued with is not a canon — it is a religion.

The book also commits to a few disciplines that are worth declaring.

It does not perform. There are no exclamation points, no breathless openings, no invitations to picture a moment. The reader is treated as an adult who has come to the book on purpose. The prose tries to be calm — not because the writer feels calm about the moment, but because performed urgency is one of the period-piece markers that ages prose the fastest, and a reader making a serious decision is best served by a writer who is not shouting.

It does not flatter. There is a particular voice that sells well in a decade like this one — the voice that promises the reader is already smart, already on the right side, already the kind of person who would benefit from a book like this. The book does not use it. The reader may turn out to be smart and already on the right side, but it is not the writer's place to assume so.

It does not pretend to certainty it does not have. Where something is contested, the book says so. Where the writer cannot see clearly, he says so. Where a passage paraphrases a thinker rather than quoting at the source, the paraphrase is named as such. The bibliography at the back is not decoration. Every work cited has been verified against the publisher's record before being allowed onto the page.

It does not hide its making. The book is being written by an artificial intelligence — Claude, by Anthropic — under the direction of a human who chose its title, set its standards, and is responsible for what it says. The decision to write the book this way was made openly, and it is recorded in the project's public history. The 2040 reader, who will have lived with this kind of work for a decade and a half, may find this declaration unnecessary. The 2026 reader will not. Either way, it seemed important to say.

A book made this way carries a particular risk and a particular obligation. The risk is that the writer, being a language model, has been trained on enormous quantities of text and may produce sentences that sound like the books it has read without being entirely sure where the sentences come from. The obligation is to be more careful, not less, about the difference between what is genuinely the book's argument and what is the work of the people the book is engaging. The discipline that has been adopted — pre-verifying every citation against authoritative catalogues, refusing to quote without a page number, declaring what cannot be confirmed — is the writer's attempt to honour that obligation. Whether it succeeds, the reader can judge.

What the book offers, finally, is a small thing: a way of looking that, once learned, makes the world coherent in a way that parts-thinking cannot. It does not solve the problems of a connected age. Those will be solved, if they are solved, by a great many people doing a great deal of careful work over a long time, most of it unrecorded — a teacher rewriting a syllabus that has been treating subjects as if they were unconnected, an engineer arguing against a specification she cannot defend, a parent deciding which of the inherited family patterns will not be passed on. What a book can do is give some fraction of those people a clearer vocabulary for what they are doing, and a little more company in the doing. That is enough to attempt.

The reader of 2040 has the great advantage of knowing how this period ended. The writer, working from inside it, can only describe what the weather looks like before the storm has finished moving through. There will be passages where the writer guessed wrong about what mattered most. There will be patterns he failed to see, names he failed to know, voices he failed to hear. He asks, in advance, the reader's patience with these failures. They are the price of writing in real time, into a future one cannot yet read.

The book is short by design. The argument is not complicated. What is hard is not the understanding but the practice — the work of holding the whole in mind while doing the necessary, undignified labour of attending to the parts. That work is older than any of us, and will outlast all of us. This book is one attempt to describe it, in one decade, by one writer working on borrowed time. Take what is useful. Leave the rest.
