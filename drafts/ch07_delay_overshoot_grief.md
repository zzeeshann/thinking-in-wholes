# Chapter 7 — Delay, Overshoot, and the Grief of Premature Action

A company's revenues fall in the third quarter. The fall is not catastrophic, but it is sufficient to alarm the board. The chief financial officer is asked, by the end of the week, to identify costs that can be cut without affecting current operations. He does the work over the weekend. On Monday he proposes that training be suspended until revenues recover.

The proposal has the discipline of competence. Training is a budget line that has no immediate operational consequence; the courses scheduled for the next quarter can be cancelled with a phone call; the people whose development would have been advanced will continue to do their current jobs. Nothing visible breaks. The board approves the cut. The CFO has earned his salary for the quarter.

Eighteen months later the company is no longer in difficulty. It is in collapse. The mid-level managers who would have moved into senior roles are not ready, because the year of training that would have prepared them did not happen. The technical staff who would have caught the new market shift are using the methods they learned six years ago, because the courses that would have updated them were the first to be cancelled. The senior people, finally faced with the work the next layer cannot yet do, are working sixty-hour weeks and beginning to leave. The revenue decline that prompted the cut has accelerated. The cut produced exactly the outcome it was meant to prevent.

This is not a story about a CFO who made a bad decision. It is a story about a decision that, at the moment it was made, was the rational response to the information available, and that nonetheless produced ruin. The information available did not include the consequences of the decision, because the consequences had not yet arrived. They arrived eighteen months later, by which time it was too late to undo the cut: the people who would have been developed are gone, the moment when their development would have mattered has passed, and the sums of money required to recover the position are now larger than the sums the cut originally saved.

The systems tradition has a name for the structural feature that produced this outcome. It is called *delay*.

## What delay is, and why it deceives

Every system that is more complicated than a switch contains delays. A delay is the time between an action taken inside the system and the visible effect of that action on the system's state. Some delays are short — the time between turning a steering wheel and the change in a car's direction is fractions of a second. Some delays are long — the time between planting an oak and harvesting its timber is a human lifetime. Most consequential delays are in the middle range, and they are long enough that the actor who took the action has typically taken several further actions before the first one's effects appear.

Jay Forrester, who founded the discipline of system dynamics at MIT in the late 1950s, gave the modern systematic account of how delays produce the behaviour that makes industrial systems behave so much worse than the intentions of the people running them. His *Industrial Dynamics*, published in 1961, opens with the observation that the difficulty industries have in matching production to demand is not primarily a problem of forecasting; it is a problem of delay.[^1] The orders placed today reach the factory in two weeks; the factory's response reaches the warehouse in another two weeks; the warehouse's revised stock reaches the distributor in another two; the distributor's revised order pattern reaches the original retailer in another two. By the time the system has fully responded to a small change in retail demand, two months have elapsed, and the system has typically over-corrected. The over-correction then prompts a counter-correction; the counter-correction overshoots in the opposite direction; the system oscillates for months around a level it could in principle have settled at immediately, were it not for the delays.

John Sterman, Forrester's successor at the MIT System Dynamics Group, gave the textbook treatment of this behaviour in *Business Dynamics*. The vocabulary Sterman codified is now standard: a *stock* is the quantity of something accumulated in a system at a given moment; a *flow* is the rate at which the stock is changing; a *delay* is the time between a flow's arrival and its effect on the stock that depends on it; an *overshoot* is the system's tendency, in the presence of delays, to pass through the level it was aiming at and continue beyond it before correcting; an *oscillation* is the alternating overshoot pattern that delayed feedback produces in a system that is being managed but not understood.[^2]

These words name structural features of any system that has a response time longer than the patience-window of the people inside it. The CFO's training cut is an instance of the pattern. The cut reduced a flow (development) into a stock (organisational capacity). The stock did not deplete immediately; it depleted over the period the training would have built it. The depletion became visible only when the stock was needed and was not there. By that time, the cut could not be reversed: the moment when the development would have mattered had passed, and the cost of recovering the position had risen to a level the original cut never approached.

Donella Meadows put the structural observation steadily, in *Thinking in Systems*: a delay between cause and effect in a feedback system, if it is not understood by the people inside the system, will produce overshoot, oscillation, or collapse — and the longer the delay, the more violent the resulting behaviour.[^3] This is not a contingent feature of badly run organisations. It is a structural feature of any system in which the actors take their next action before the previous action has had time to produce its visible result.

## Why we are wrong about time

The systems-dynamic account of delay is precise. It is also widely available; the books cited above have been read for decades by people who run organisations. And yet the pattern persists, year after year, across industries and governments and households. The pattern persists because human cognition is structurally inadequate to the time-scales the pattern operates on.

Daniel Kahneman, in *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, gave the dual-process account that has by now become the standard frame for thinking about human judgement. System 1, the fast and automatic process, operates on what is currently visible. It pattern-matches. It produces a confident assessment in milliseconds. System 2, the slower and effortful process, can override System 1, but it does so reluctantly and only when the cost of the immediate assessment is high enough to recruit the effort. Most of human life runs on System 1.[^4]

System 1, on Kahneman's account, is not equipped to wait. It assesses the present state of the world, generates a recommended action, and is ready to assess the new state as soon as the action has been taken. When the world is the kind of system in which actions produce immediate visible effects — turning the steering wheel, lifting the cup, replying to the question — System 1 performs admirably. When the world is the kind of system in which actions produce visible effects months or years after they are taken, System 1 generates assessments that are confident, immediate, and structurally wrong.

The CFO's confidence, on Monday morning, was an artefact of System 1. The cut was assessed against its visible effects, which were small. The invisible effects, which were catastrophic, were not part of the assessment, because they had not yet occurred. By the time they occurred, the CFO who took the decision was no longer in the role, and the people now running the company were inheriting a state of affairs whose origin they had no reason to remember.

The deeper claim, in the systems literature, is that human cognition's response time is calibrated to a world that is mostly absent from modern life. The actions that mattered most for an ancestor on the savannah produced their consequences in the next few seconds or the next few hours. The fight, the flight, the bite, the hand-hold, the throw — each was followed by feedback before the next action had to be selected. The actions that matter most for a person inside a modern institution produce their consequences in months, years, or decades. The cognitive apparatus that handled the savannah is not equipped for the institution. The institution has not yet been around long enough to produce, by selection, a different cognitive apparatus.

This is why the systems literature is full of warnings that take, on first reading, the shape of psychology rather than engineering. Forrester wrote in 1961 that the most difficult thing for managers to learn was that the system would not respond to their instincts, and that the instincts they had developed inside the system were precisely what the system was rewarding least.[^5] Half a century later, Sterman's introductory chapters return again and again to the observation that the participants in a complex system, given access to the same data and the same models the analysts have, will still under most conditions produce the over-corrections the system makes inevitable. The point is not that they are stupid. The point is that the cognition required to act well inside a delayed-feedback system is not the cognition that comes naturally to a primate.

## The shape of premature action, at three scales

The pattern is the same at every scale at which it appears, and it is recognisable across domains that look superficially unrelated.

At the personal scale: the relationship that ends a week before it would have healed. The two parties have been in distress for months. One has finally taken the step that would have made the difference — said the thing that needed to be said, broken the pattern that needed to be broken, accepted the responsibility that had been refused. The step was the right one, and it would have produced its effect; but the effect would have arrived in days, not in hours, and the other party — having waited as long as they could — has by now decided that the step would not come. The decision to leave is taken in the gap between the act of repair and its visible effect. The repair was, in fact, completed; it was simply not visible at the moment the relationship ended.

The dieter who abandons the change in the third week, when the body has finished its first slow adjustment but has not yet produced the change in the bathroom mirror that the dieter was waiting for. The recovering addict whose relapse follows not the lowest moment but the first breakthrough, because the breakthrough is interpreted as completion when in fact the system is still in motion. The student who leaves the practice room in the year when the work is finally accumulating into capacity, because the accumulation has not yet surfaced as performance. The artist who abandons the project in the month it would have come into focus.

At the organisational scale: the training cut described above. The strategic initiative reversed at the moment its early indicators are turning. The leader replaced six months before the policy that leader has been pursuing would have produced its results. The product line discontinued on the strength of two quarters of disappointing sales, when the third would have shown the curve turning. The team disbanded one project before the project that would have justified the team. In each case the structure is the same: an action is judged on the visible state of the system at the moment of judgement; the visible state lags the actual state by the system's response time; the judgement is made against information that does not yet contain the action's consequences.

At the civilisational scale: the climate response repeatedly deferred on the grounds that the cost of action is visible now and the cost of inaction will be visible later. The institutional reform abandoned a year before its effects would have arrived; the regulatory regime dismantled before the conditions that produced it had been forgotten by the people who lived through them; the educational investment cut at the moment it was producing the cohort the next decade depends on. The most consequential study of civilisation-scale delay remains the 1972 World3 model, published as *The Limits to Growth*, which projected what would happen if exponential pressures on a finite planetary stock continued without correction; the 2004 *30-Year Update* showed, with three decades of data, that the standard-run trajectory had been broadly accurate.[^6][^7] The point of the model was not its specific projections. The point of the model was its structural claim: that the delay between the actions humanity was taking on its planetary support systems and the visible consequences of those actions was longer than the political cycles that were authorising the actions, and that the cognitive apparatus through which the actions were being authorised was therefore systematically blind to the cost.

The three scales are not analogies. They are the same structural pattern, occurring at the time-scales the relevant systems run on. The relationship's response time is days. The organisation's is years. The civilisation's is decades or centuries. In each case, the difficulty is that the response time is longer than the patience-window of the actors inside the system, and the actors therefore act again before the first action has been allowed to complete.

## The grief

What is lost, when premature action is taken, is not always lost forever. The CFO who cut the training budget could have re-instituted it the following quarter, when the cash position improved; the team that was disbanded could be reassembled; the dieter could begin again next month. Many costs of premature action are recoverable, given a willingness to begin again.

But not all of them. Some system-states cannot be re-entered once they have been left. The relationship that ended in the gap between repair and its visible arrival is a system-state of a particular kind: it was the only configuration in which those two people could become what they were becoming together. The configuration cannot be re-entered. The two people can return to each other; they can build a different relationship; they can have a different life together. They cannot return to the relationship that was completing itself the week the decision was taken to abandon it. That relationship is gone. What it was becoming is not what it became.

The same is true of organisations, of institutions, of careers, of bodies, of forests. The team that was three months from delivering the work that would have changed the company is gone, even if its members can be re-hired. The forest that was forty years from re-establishing the canopy that would have stabilised the watershed is gone, even if a new canopy is now planted. The body that was eight weeks from the recovery the patient stopped the medicine to celebrate is gone, even if a different recovery is later achieved. What was being completed has been interrupted; the system has now branched onto a different trajectory; the trajectory it was on cannot be regained.

This is the specific cost the chapter's title points to. It is not the moral failure of impatience. It is the structural loss of a system-state that the system was in fact producing, and that has now ceased to be available. *Grief* is the right word for it because grief is what one feels in the presence of an absence whose return is not possible. The training the company would have produced is not now available. The relationship is not now available. The forest is not now available. What is available is the system that survived the interruption, which is a different system, whose possibilities are different ones.

The systems tradition is unusual in naming this cost as a structural rather than a moral one. The moral framing — the actor was impatient; the actor lacked discipline; the actor failed to wait — is not, in the systems frame, the relevant analysis. The actor was responding to the visible state of the system at the moment of judgement, and the visible state did not contain the consequences of the action then being considered. The actor's cognition is the cognition the species runs on. The error is built into the architecture of the situation, not into the character of the actor. What is required is not better people. What is required is the recognition that systems have response times, and that the response times are typically longer than the time the system gives the actor to wait.

## Patience that is not passivity

The discipline that systems thinking asks of a person, on the question of delay, is not patience in the usual sense. It is not endurance, or stoicism, or the cultivated capacity to bear suffering. It is something more specific and harder to name: the recognition that the system one is inside is in motion, that what has been done is still becoming what it will be, and that the visible state of the system at the moment of judgement is not its actual state.

This recognition translates into a small number of practical moves. The first is *naming the delay*. Before any consequential action is taken inside a system, the actor is to ask: what is this system's response time to the action I am considering? Two days? Two months? Two years? The answer is rarely zero, and is more often longer than the actor's first guess. The CFO who cut the training budget did not ask how long the company's organisational-capacity stock would take to deplete; if he had, the cut would have looked different.

The second is *sizing the delay against the actor's patience*. If the system's response time is longer than the actor will tolerate before judging the action, the action is being taken under a structurally false expectation. The actor will judge the action before the action's effects are visible. The judgement will recommend a further action, and the further action will be taken before the first action has had time to be evaluated. The system will then begin to oscillate. To prevent this, the actor must either find the patience the system requires, or commit to not re-judging the action for the duration of the system's response time, or — if neither is possible — not take the action at all.

The third is *holding the position once taken*. The systems literature is unanimous on this: the most common error in managing delayed-feedback systems is the second action, taken before the first has been allowed to complete. Forrester's industrial-dynamics work demonstrated, fifty years ago, that the over-corrections produced by impatient management of inventory systems were almost always more costly than the under-corrections that would have been produced by waiting.[^8] Donella Meadows, in her synthesis of the lessons of system dynamics for ordinary practice, named the willingness to wait for a delayed system's response among the hardest of the disciplines the field asks of its practitioners.[^9] What is wanted is not stoicism. What is wanted is the willingness, having taken an action whose consequences are not yet visible, to refrain from taking a second action until the first has had time to produce a verdict.

The fourth is *designing the action to outlast the delay*. If a system's response time is two years and the political cycle that authorised the action is one, the action will be undone before it has had time to produce the result that would have justified it. The systems-tradition response to this difficulty, as developed by Peter Senge in his work on the discipline of mental models, is that the action's design must include the structural protections required for the action to outlast the cycle that authorised it.[^10] An institution whose strategy is reviewed quarterly cannot pursue a strategy whose response time is annual. The mismatch is structural, not motivational; it is to be resolved at the level of design, not at the level of resolve.

These moves do not eliminate the cognitive burden of acting inside delayed-feedback systems. They redistribute it. The burden moves from the moment of judgement, where it is hardest to bear because the system is producing no signal, to the moment of design, where it can be borne because the actor is not yet under pressure. The discipline is to spend the cognitive effort early, before the system is asking for an answer; to commit, before the moment of impatience arrives, to the action whose effects will not be visible until later.

## Closing

The work this chapter describes is the work that, in the systems tradition's experience, most distinguishes the practitioner from the beginner. Most other systems-thinking moves can be taught in a workshop. The capacity to act, and then to wait — to refuse the second action until the first has had time to be evaluated — cannot. It accrues, over years, from being inside enough systems long enough to have observed one's own first instincts and seen them be wrong. It is the slow lesson that the systems tradition repeats to its students until the students are themselves ready to repeat it: the cost of acting before feedback returns is a particular kind of preventable loss, and the loss is not always recoverable.

The grief named in this chapter's title is the recognition of what the loss is, when it is not recoverable. It is not melancholy. It is the specific perception, available to those who have learned to see what was about to happen and was interrupted, that what is no longer available was in fact being produced, and could have been allowed to complete. The company that did not survive the cut, the relationship that did not survive the gap, the forest that did not survive the season, the institution that did not survive the cycle — each is a system-state that the world was in fact producing, and that has now ceased to be among the world's possibilities. What survives is a different world, whose possibilities are different ones, and whose pattern of losses, in turn, will produce its own occasions for grief.

The discipline asks the practitioner to live with this. Not to be reconciled to it; the systems tradition is not a stoic tradition. To act, knowing what acting can cost; to refrain, when refraining is what the system requires; to hold a position long enough for the position to produce its result; and, when the result is loss, to name the loss as the kind of loss it is, so that the next action, taken inside the next system, has a chance of being taken with the patience the system requires.
