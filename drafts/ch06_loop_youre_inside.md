# Chapter 6 — The Loop You're Inside

A marriage proceeds in this way. When stress arrives — work, money, illness, the small and large pressures of a shared life — one partner moves toward the other and the other moves away. The one who moves toward calls it concern, calls it the natural thing to do, says he is trying to keep them connected. The one who moves away calls it space, calls it what she needs in order to think, says she is trying to keep them functional. Each can describe in careful detail why the other's behaviour is the problem. Each story is internally coherent, each is supported by years of evidence, and each is true to the partner who tells it. The marriage gets worse.

It gets worse because the stress that causes the move toward and the move away does not pass; it returns, and each round, the move toward is a little more anxious because the move away of the previous round was longer, and the move away is a little more decisive because the move toward was more pressing. From inside the marriage, what is happening is two people behaving badly, or — more often, more painfully — two people behaving as well as they can while the other behaves badly. From outside the marriage, what is happening is one structure. It is a pattern, named in the older counselling literature as the pursuer-distancer cycle, and it is doing what its structure determines it to do, which is to amplify itself with each round.

The shape of the pattern is what the systems literature calls a reinforcing loop. The behaviour of one party causes the behaviour of the other, which causes more of the original behaviour in the first, which causes more in the second; round and round, with each round larger or sharper or more entrenched than the last. Reinforcing loops do not return to where they started. They drift, or escalate, or collapse. They are the engines of growth and the engines of decline, and the same loop, depending on which way it is turning, will look like prosperity or like ruin.[^1]

A balancing loop, by contrast, is a loop with a target. The thermostat is the standard textbook example. The room cools below the set point; the heater runs; the room warms above the set point; the heater stops. The system oscillates around its target, dampens, and settles. Balancing loops produce stability. They are responsible for most of what works in the body, in well-functioning households, in well-run institutions. They are why a healthy system is a quieter system than the news suggests, and they are usually invisible because their presence registers as the normal absence of crisis.

The reinforcing kind is the kind that produces the lived experience of being trapped — of being inside a loop one cannot see one is inside.

## Why the loop feels like fate

The most important property of a reinforcing loop, from the point of view of the person inside it, is that the loop does not announce itself as a loop. It announces itself as reality.

The pursuer in the marriage does not perceive himself as a pursuer in a structural pattern; he perceives the distance between himself and his wife. The distancer does not perceive herself as a distancer in a structural pattern; she perceives the pressure of the pursuit. Each perceives the other as the cause and the self as the response. This perception is not a moral failing; it is what the loop looks like from inside it, in the same way that the surface of the earth looks flat from a chair on a porch. The geometry that would correct the perception is not available at the scale at which the perception is occurring.

This is why a loop, undiagnosed, feels like fate, like personality, like incompatibility, like the way things are. The observation is steady in Donella Meadows's writing: a system gives the behaviour it is structured to give, and the people inside it experience that behaviour as their own circumstances rather than as the output of a structure they are part of.[^2] The hospital that cannot stop crashing through its night shifts experiences itself as poorly staffed, badly led, or simply unlucky. The school that cannot stop losing its best teachers experiences itself as a victim of the labour market or the parents. The country that cannot stop polarising experiences itself as the casualty of bad people on the other side. Each is, in fact, in a loop. Each loop is doing what its structure requires. The participants, inside the loop, see a world doing things to them.

The shift the systems tradition asks for, at this point, is not a moral one but a perceptual one. It is the move from the question *what is happening to me?* to the question *what pattern am I inside?* The first question yields a story, often a true story as far as it goes, about the world's mistreatment of the speaker. The second yields a structure, which is the story's necessary condition. Without the structure, the story could not have arisen; with the structure, that story or some version of it was almost guaranteed.

## Why pushing harder fails

Once a loop is in motion, the most natural human response is to do more of what one is already doing.

The pursuer, sensing distance, pursues harder. The distancer, sensing pursuit, distances further. The manager whose team is underperforming supervises the team more closely; the team, supervised, becomes more cautious, less willing to use its judgement, less able to perform; the manager supervises more closely still. The state whose neighbour is arming arms in response; the neighbour, watching the arming, accelerates its own; both states explain to themselves and to history that they had no choice. The school whose test scores have fallen drills the students harder on the test; the drilling crowds out the kinds of teaching that produce the underlying competence the test was originally designed to measure; the scores fall further; the drilling intensifies.

In each case the response that feels most reasonable from inside the loop is the response that tightens the loop. This is among the hardest of the systems tradition's lessons to absorb, because it overturns the moral grammar of effort. We are taught to try harder. We are taught that the response to a failing strategy is renewed application of it. We are not taught — though every parent of a teenager has the data — that the renewed application is the failure. Peter Senge gave the observation a blunter form for institutions. A system whose feedback the participant has not understood does not yield to harder pushing; it resists more sharply, and the resistance is structural, not malicious.[^3]

This is the meaning of the older folk observation that a person can be his own worst enemy and the meaning of the newer institutional one that an organisation can be its own. Neither is metaphor. Each names the structural fact that a loop's most committed participant is its most reliable engine. The harder one rows, the faster one moves, even when one is rowing in a circle.

## At three scales

A loop does not respect the boundary between a life and a career, or between a career and a civilisation. The same shape recurs.

At the personal scale: the man who works long hours because he fears for his place, and whose long hours leave him exhausted, and whose exhaustion shows up as poor judgement at work, and whose poor judgement gives him fresh reason to fear for his place. He works longer hours. The loop turns. He arrives, after some years, at the conclusion that he was correct to fear; everyone around him agrees; the firing, when it comes, retroactively justifies a decade of overwork. The loop has produced its prediction. It always does.

At the organisational scale: a company that has been losing customers responds with a drive to acquire new customers. The drive is expensive and consumes the time that would otherwise have been spent on the customers it still has. Service to existing customers degrades. Existing customers leave. The acquisition target rises. The drive becomes more expensive. The board, asked at the year's end what is wrong, is told that the market is harder than it once was, and that competitors have grown sharper. This is true at one level. At another level, the market is harder because the company is in a loop that converts a manageable churn rate into an unmanageable one through its own response to it.

At the civilisational scale: an arms race; a regulatory ratchet; a polarisation. The patterns are old. Two political parties, each defining itself increasingly by opposition to the other, drift outward; each move outward by one provokes a corresponding move outward by the other; each side's evidence for the other's extremism is the move the other has just made in response to the previous move. The loop runs for a generation. Each side describes itself as defensive and the other as causal, and each is, locally, correct. The loop has no agent except the actors in it, and the actors cannot see, until the loop is far advanced, that they have been in conversation with their own behaviour.

The personal loop is not analogous to the civilisational one. They are the same thing. The systems tradition's most consequential claim is that the loop is the unit of behaviour at every scale, and that the techniques for seeing it, naming it, and interrupting it transfer across the scales because the structure does. This is what makes the discipline a discipline rather than a domain.

## Naming a loop

The first work is to make the loop visible.

This is harder than it sounds because nothing in the loop's daily operation requires the loop to be named, and nothing in the loop's daily operation rewards the naming. The loop runs on the responses of its participants, and the participants' responses are all furnished with their own justifications. Each move feels caused by the previous move of the other party, and not by the loop's structure. The work of naming the loop is the work of suspending, briefly, the explanation one prefers, and asking instead what would happen if one kept doing exactly what one is doing.

A few questions, asked in sequence, will surface most of the reinforcing loops a person is in. *What pattern have I noticed, over months or years, that recurs?* *What is my customary response to it?* *What is the response of the others involved?* *What does my response do to their response, and theirs to mine?* *If I were to plot the round across several iterations, would I be in the same place, or would the situation be drifting?* If the answer to the last question is that it is drifting — getting larger, getting worse, getting more fixed — the loop is reinforcing, and the questions before it have produced its sketch.

The systems literature has a small library of common reinforcing patterns, named so that they can be recognised. *Escalation*, in which two parties match each other's intensity and each round is sharper than the last. *Shifting the burden*, in which a symptom is treated by a quick response that both relieves the symptom and erodes the system's capacity for the slower response that would address the cause. *Drift to low performance*, in which a system whose results have declined responds by lowering its standard of acceptable performance, then declines again to the new lower standard, then lowers again. *Tragedy of the commons*, in which a shared resource is consumed by many actors whose individual incentives lead each to take a little more, until the resource fails. *Limits to growth*, in which a process that has been growing on a reinforcing loop encounters a balancing loop it had not noticed and stalls; the participants, accustomed to the growth, push harder on the original lever, which intensifies the balancing loop and accelerates the failure.[^4] These are not all the patterns, but they are the ones a literate observer will see most often, and recognising them is most of the practical art of seeing loops.

## Where the leverage is

Once a loop is named, the question is how to interrupt it. This is where the systems tradition departs most sharply from common sense.

Common sense says to push harder where one has been pushing, or — recognising that the pushing is the problem — to push somewhere new with the same vigour. The systems tradition says that the leverage is rarely in the obvious place, and the obvious place is rarely a place of leverage. Donella Meadows, in the late 1990s, set out a list of the kinds of intervention that influence a system, ordered roughly by their effect.[^5] The lowest-leverage intervention, she argued, is to change the parameters: budget allocations, staffing ratios, tax rates, the dials that institutions love to turn. Slightly higher: changing the size of stocks, the buffers and reserves a system holds. Higher still: changing the structure of the feedback loops themselves, including the delays inside them. Higher: changing the rules under which the system operates. Higher: changing the goals of the system, the purpose against which it is measuring itself. Highest: changing the paradigm out of which the system's goals arise — the unstated assumptions about what the system is for, which the participants take to be reality.

The list is not a recipe; it is a hierarchy of where to look. The reason it inverts intuition is that the obvious dials — the parameters at the bottom of the list — are obvious because they are where the participants have decided the system can be controlled. The participants have decided wrong. The dials they are turning are the dials least connected to the loop's actual behaviour. The leverage is upstream, where the rules and the goals and the assumptions sit, and these are the points the participants do not see because the participants are produced by them.

A loop is interrupted, in practice, by a change at one of the higher-leverage points. The marriage's pursuer-distancer cycle is rarely solved by either partner pursuing or distancing better. It is solved, when it is, by a change in the rule under which both have been operating — usually a rule that one of them did not know was a rule, about what closeness is supposed to feel like, or about what one of them owes the other in any given week. The school's drilling-and-decline loop is not solved by better drilling. It is solved by a change in what the school is measuring itself against, which is to say in what the school is for. The arms race is not solved by either side's better armament. It is solved, when it is, by a change in the rules of the game both states are playing, or by an exhaustion of the paradigm that produced the game in the first place.

## Interrupting a loop

The practice of interruption is, in its quietest form, the practice of one participant stopping, even briefly, the move the loop requires.

David Stroh, writing about the application of systems thinking to social change, observed that the moment of interruption is rarely a confrontation; it is more often a refusal.[^6] A refusal to play the next round of the move that has been played a thousand times. The pursuer notices the urge to pursue and waits. The distancer notices the urge to retreat and stays. The manager notices the urge to supervise more closely and asks instead what the team would do if left alone for an afternoon. The school notices the urge to drill and asks instead what the children would do with an hour and a problem that did not have an answer. None of these is dramatic. Each is the system, deprived of its accustomed input, beginning to settle into a different shape.

The settling is not always benign. A loop interrupted is a system without its familiar feedback, and the parties may, at first, intensify the very behaviour the interruption was meant to address. The distancer who finds himself suddenly not pursued may retreat further, into the silence; the team, suddenly unsupervised, may use the autonomy badly for some weeks before learning to use it well; the school, suddenly not drilling, may see scores fall before any other measure of formation rises. The interruption requires patience because the loop has been doing its work for a long time and the system has organised its expectations around its operation. Allowing the system to find a new equilibrium is the discipline. Pushing harder, again, in any direction, is the loop, in its new form, recovering.

This is why the work of interrupting a loop is, often, the work of one person. The change that destabilises the system rarely comes from all parties simultaneously deciding to behave differently; it comes from one participant choosing, against the run of the situation, to make a move that the loop did not call for. The choice is small. Its consequences are sometimes large. The systems literature has a name for the participants who do this — it varies, in different traditions — but the position they occupy is always the same. They are the people who have stopped explaining the loop and started altering it.

## What the loop has been all along

A reinforcing loop is the smallest unit of behaviour over time. Most of what is hard in a life — the patterns one cannot break, the relationships one cannot fix, the projects one cannot finish — has a loop at its structural heart. Most of what is hard in an organisation has a loop at its structural heart. Most of what is hard in a civilisation, including the failures that will be visible by 2040 to anyone who has the eyes for them, has a loop at its structural heart. To learn to see loops is to learn to see the structure of one's own difficulty.

It is also to acquire a particular kind of patience. The participant who has named the loop knows that the move that feels most pressing — the harder push, the next round of pursuit, the renewed attempt to make the part work — is the loop calling for its accustomed input, and that the input will produce its accustomed output. Refusing the input is not weakness or passivity. It is the only move available that is not the loop. It is also, almost always, the move that no one else is going to make, because no one else has seen what one is now seeing.

To live well, in a connected world, is in large part to recognise the loops one is inside, to refuse to be their reliable engine, and to stay in the system long enough for the new equilibrium to find itself.
