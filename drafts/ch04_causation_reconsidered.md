# Chapter 4 — Causation Reconsidered

A field after a windy autumn. An oak drops thousands of acorns. A handful germinate. Of the seedlings, some survive a winter; fewer survive a decade; one in a million acorns becomes an old tree. Asked what caused that tree, the obvious answer is the acorn. But the acorn alone, sealed in a glass jar in a desk drawer, becomes nothing. It does not cause an oak. It cannot.

The acorn is necessary. It is not sufficient. For an oak to appear, the acorn requires soil it can root in, water it can draw, light it can reach, freedom from the squirrels that would eat it and the deer that would graze its early leaves, and a long enough run of survivable years for the slow work of growth. The acorn is one element in a configuration. The configuration produces.

This is the small philosophical correction behind much of the systems tradition. The relation between an acorn and an oak is not cause and effect. It is *producer and product*. The producer makes the product possible; the producer does not, on its own, make the product actual. Whether the product appears depends on conditions outside the producer that have to be satisfied for the production to run.

The chapter that follows is about what the small correction changes. It changes the question one asks of a finished outcome. It changes the question one asks of an unfinished situation in which an outcome is being sought. It changes the question one asks when something has gone wrong and the temptation is to find the person who did it. Once the changes are seen, the analytic habit of asking *what caused this?* stops looking like the level of rigour. It starts looking like the residue of a metaphysical commitment that has not been examined.

## The inherited model

The metaphysical commitment came down through the same lineage Chapter 1 traced. The mechanical philosophy of the seventeenth century treated nature as matter in motion, and matter in motion as governed by clean rules of contact and force. A billiard ball, struck by a cue, transmits the cue's energy through its centre of mass and travels in a determinate line until the next contact. Each event has a single immediate antecedent. Each antecedent has a single consequence. To explain any event in such a world is to find the antecedent that produced it; to control any outcome is to alter the antecedent; to study any phenomenon is to isolate it from the noise that would obscure the line of cause.

Newton's mechanics gave this picture mathematical force, and the force ran outward through three centuries of European thought. By the late nineteenth century the working assumption of educated practice in nearly every domain was that the world consisted of separable events, each with its proper cause; that finding the cause was the work of science; and that finding the cause sufficed to explain. The assumption produced an enormous quantity of useful knowledge. It worked spectacularly in mechanics, in chemistry, in much of physiology. It was the methodological inheritance the analytic tradition carried into the twentieth century.

Where it failed was where the events would not separate. A patient is unwell. A child is unhappy. A city is fraying. A company is failing. In each case the search for *the* cause produces, almost without effort, half a dozen plausible candidates, and the examination of any one of them produces an account that explains some of the situation and leaves the rest unexplained. The pattern is not failure of inquiry. It is failure of the framework in which inquiry is being conducted. The framework expects single causes for events that do not have them.

The framework persists, despite its known failures, because the institutions that grew up around it inherited its expectations. Courts ask for *the* proximate cause; insurance contracts pay against *the* cause of loss; accident inquiries identify *the* root cause; clinical reasoning, in much of its inherited training, looks for *the* pathogen, *the* lesion, *the* deficit. In each case the institution has been built to accept a single answer, and the practitioner who returns instead with a configuration is judged not to have done the inquiry. The institutional sediment of the older model is part of why the correction is slow even where the correction is well known. The vocabulary of single cause is built into the forms.

## Singer's revision

The figure who saw this clearly, from a position inside professional philosophy and from a tradition the analytic culture had not bothered to refute, was Edgar A. Singer Jr. Singer taught philosophy at the University of Pennsylvania across the first half of the twentieth century; he stood in the experimentalist tradition descended from William James; and he was, through his student C. West Churchman and through Churchman's student Russell Ackoff, the philosophical source of much of what later became the systems tradition in management.[^1] His writing is now seldom read directly. It is worth the trouble.

Singer's revision was small in formulation and large in implication. He observed that the model of cause inherited from the mechanical philosophy described a relation in which an event was both *necessary* and *sufficient* for what followed it. The cue strike, on the billiard ball, is necessary for the ball's motion and, given the ball's mass and the table's friction, also sufficient for it. Almost no event in the world outside an idealised mechanics is like this. The acorn is necessary for the oak; it is not sufficient. The wound is necessary for the infection that follows; it is not sufficient. The argument is necessary for the divorce; it is not sufficient. In every interesting case the antecedent makes the consequent possible without making it actual; the actuality requires conditions whose presence is, from the antecedent's standpoint, contingent.[^2]

Singer named this relation the producer-product relation and treated it as the basic relation of empirical inquiry, with simple causation as a limiting case. A producer is what makes a product possible. The product becomes actual when the producer is joined by the contributory conditions the producer cannot itself supply. The contributory conditions are real elements of the situation; they are not noise to be averaged away. A theory of an outcome that does not name them is not a complete theory. It has named a producer and pretended it had named a cause.

Three implications follow, each consequential. The first is that explanation by single antecedent is, except in idealised cases, a category error. To explain an oak by its acorn is to confuse a necessary condition with the configuration that produced. The second is that prediction by single antecedent is unreliable in the same way: knowing the producer is not enough to forecast the product, because the contributory conditions may or may not appear. The third — and this is where Singer's lineage entered the systems tradition — is that intervention by single antecedent will succeed only when the contributory conditions are already in place and reliably maintained. Where they are not, intervening at the producer is theatre.

Naming the contributory conditions is therefore the practical discipline the analytic culture had let atrophy. To name them well is harder than it looks. The conditions are often invisible until something fails; they include elements the analyst would prefer not to name, because naming them makes the analyst responsible for them; they run across boundaries the analyst has been trained to respect. The contributory conditions of a patient's illness include the patient's family, the patient's neighbourhood, the patient's water supply, the patient's economic position; an analysis that respects the boundary at the patient's skin will miss most of them. The discipline is inseparable from the willingness to redraw boundaries — a problem introduced in the previous chapter and now re-encountered as the structural cost of taking causation seriously.

Singer's late writings, gathered after his death by Churchman in *Experience and Reflection* in 1959, work the producer-product relation through into a general account of empirical knowledge.[^3] The central claim is that knowledge of a system requires knowledge of both its producers and its contributory conditions, and that the second of these — the conditions — is what the analytic tradition had taught its students to overlook.

## The Ackoff and Emery deepening

What Singer left as a philosophical position, his student's student turned into a working method. Ackoff, with the Australian psychologist Fred Emery, set out in *On Purposeful Systems* in 1972 to develop the producer-product relation into the formal apparatus of a behavioural science.[^4] The book is dense and not widely read; its central construction, however, is easy to state.

Ackoff and Emery defined a *purposeful* system as one capable of producing the same outcome by different means and different outcomes in the same environment. The capacity is the test. A thermostat, given a room, produces a temperature; change the room and it produces the same temperature by the same means. A purposeful system, given a room, can produce a temperature, can produce a different temperature if it chooses, can choose to keep the temperature varying for some other reason, and can change its means as the room changes. The thermostat has a behaviour. The purposeful system has *purposes* — in the older sense of ends it can adopt, examine, and revise.

This is where the producer-product distinction does its serious work. For a purposeful system, the question of what produced an outcome has two answers, and neither is a single antecedent. The outer answer is the configuration of contributory conditions in which the outcome became possible; the inner answer is the choice the system made among its available means. The inner answer is what makes a system purposeful at all. Without it, the system is mechanical, in the strict Newtonian sense, and its outcomes are calculable from its inputs. With it, the outcomes are not calculable; they are made.

The implication for any inquiry into a purposeful system is that the question of what produced an outcome is also, inseparably, a question of what the system was trying to bring about. A theory of the system's behaviour that does not include its purposes is not just incomplete; it is the theory of a different kind of object. The mechanical model treats the system as a thermostat. The system is not a thermostat. Treating it as one produces the now-familiar diagnostic failures: the corporate restructuring that fixes nothing because it took the workforce as inputs to be optimised, the policy intervention that fails because it took the public as a population to be steered, the educational reform that fails because it took the teachers as instruments rather than as professionals choosing among available means.[^5]

## Multiple producers, contributory conditions

The producer-product distinction has a further consequence the older model could not accommodate. Most outcomes worth explaining have more than one producer.

A patient with a chronic illness has a genetic predisposition, a sustained stress at work, a diet shaped by what was available in childhood, a microbiome altered by an antibiotic course in their twenties, an air quality near their home that fluctuates with the season, and a sleep pattern set by their household. To ask which of these caused the illness is to mistake the situation. Each is a producer; the illness is the product of their joint operation; removing any one of them changes the probability of the illness, but no one of them, alone, produced it. Treatment that addresses only one is treatment that has accepted the inherited model's framing of the question. Sometimes — when the others are stable enough — it works. Often it does not, and the failure is taken as a failure of the treatment, when it is the framing that failed.

What the producer-product analysis recommends, in such a case, is not the abandonment of treatment. It is the abandonment of the assumption that any single intervention will be the answer. A serious treatment plan addresses several producers at once, accepting that the relative weight of each is uncertain and watching for which ones, when modified, change the trajectory. The plan succeeds not because any one element of it was the cause, but because the joint modification shifts the configuration toward a different attractor. The patient may not be able to say which change made the difference, and the practitioner who has read the situation correctly will not need to. The configuration produced the illness; a different configuration is what would produce its remission. The same logic applies, with adjustments for scale, to the design of public-health policy, to the reform of an organisation, and to the slower work of changing one's own life.

The same pattern obtains in every domain the systems tradition takes seriously. A forest fire requires fuel, dryness, ignition, and wind. A bank run requires a triggering event, a pre-existing fragility, and a network through which alarm propagates faster than reassurance. A book that fails requires neither bad sentences nor a bad subject — it requires, usually, a misalignment between what the writer was trying to bring about and what the structure could deliver. In each case the search for *the* cause is a misuse of language; the inquiry one needs is into the configuration of contributory conditions, and the relative weight of each within it.

This is what Meadows meant by her insistence that behaviour comes out of structure.[^6] The structure of a system is the standing configuration of its producers and the relations among them. The behaviour is the product. A structural change — a change to which producers are present and how they are joined — produces a behavioural change. A change to the producers' settings, without a change to the structure, produces, almost always, behaviour reverting to its prior pattern within a few cycles. The system is doing what its structure produces it to do.

## The ethics of explanation

The producer-product correction has its hardest consequence in the practice of blame. When something has gone wrong — a patient dies, a project fails, a marriage ends, a market crashes — the analytic habit is to find the person responsible. The habit has a moral weight to it: it expresses the conviction that human action matters, that responsibility is real, that systems of accountability are how civilisations protect themselves against the worst behaviour of their members. The conviction is not wrong. The habit, as practised, is.

Blame names a producer. It says, of an outcome, that this person did this thing, and the thing produced what followed. The naming is often correct in the limited sense the inherited model allows: the act did, in fact, occur, and the outcome did, in fact, follow it. What the naming omits is the configuration of contributory conditions inside which the act could have the consequence it did. The surgeon who made the wrong cut had been on shift for nineteen hours because the rota was short-staffed because the previous administration had cut training budgets because the budget cycle rewarded short-run efficiency over long-run capacity. The investor who triggered the run had been able to do so because the institution's reserves had been thinned over fifteen years of optimisation against a measure that had been chosen for its tractability rather than its meaning. In each case the producer is real. So are the conditions. Naming only the producer is taking a single line out of a structural account and offering it as the whole.

The practical effect of treating blame as explanation is the one anyone who has watched institutions fail recognises. The named person is removed; the conditions remain; the next occupant of the role, in the same configuration, produces the same outcome, often within months. Repeat. The institution now has a sequence of named producers and a stable pattern of bad outcomes, and every cycle of removal confirms, to the observers who treat blame as explanation, that bad people keep ending up in the role. The diagnosis is upside down. The role is producing the people. The configuration is producing the outcomes. The producers can be replaced indefinitely without effect.

This is not an argument against accountability. Accountability is one of the contributory conditions a humane system requires; without it, the system's purposeful members lose the capacity to choose well, because the consequences of choosing badly have been removed from view. The argument is against the substitution of accountability for diagnosis. Naming the producer is a moral act; it is not, on its own, an explanatory one; and a culture that has confused the two will find itself holding many people responsible and changing nothing.

The systems tradition's discipline is to hold both at once. The configuration is what produced the outcome. The person who acted within the configuration is also responsible for what they did. Both statements are true. The honesty the tradition demands is to make both, in their proper proportion, in any account of why something has gone wrong — and to prefer, when an institution can be redesigned rather than re-staffed, the redesign.[^7]

What designed accountability looks like, in practical detail, is the inheritance of the systems tradition's organisational work. Ackoff's idealised design, treated in Chapter 2, is a method for redesigning the configuration that produces the outcomes. Beer's viable system model, traced in the same chapter, gives the structural account of what an institution requires in order to remain coherent under change. Both methods take seriously what blame-as-explanation cannot: that the institution is itself a producer, that the persons inside it are operating within the conditions the institution sets, and that changing the outcomes requires changing the conditions. The accountability designed into such a system is not the search for the person to remove; it is the standing arrangement by which information about consequence flows back to the persons whose choices produced it, in time for them to choose differently. This is a stronger discipline than blame, because it requires the institution to know what it is doing and to confront the knowledge. Most institutions, given the choice, prefer blame.

## What the formal apparatus can and cannot do

A note is owed here on the most-discussed development in the philosophy of causation in the early twenty-first century — the formal-statistical apparatus associated with the computer scientist Judea Pearl and worked out for a wider audience in *The Book of Why*, written with Dana Mackenzie in 2018.[^8] Pearl's apparatus is significant. It gives a clean mathematical treatment of the difference between correlation and causation, a vocabulary for distinguishing observational from interventional from counterfactual reasoning, and a method for inferring causal structure from data when the assumptions of the method are met.

The apparatus does not, however, rescue the inherited model. It refines and disciplines it. Pearl's framework operates within the producer-product world Singer described: causal inference, on Pearl's account, requires assumptions about the structure of the situation that have to come from outside the data — from the analyst's prior understanding of what could plausibly produce what, in the configuration being studied. The framework is a rigorous tool for working out the implications of a structural model. It is not a method for arriving at the structural model without judgement.

For practical purposes the implication is that the formal apparatus is most useful exactly where the systems tradition's qualitative discipline has done its work: where the producers have been identified, the contributory conditions named, and the structure drawn. In that context, Pearl's methods produce sharper estimates of the relative weight of each producer than older statistical methods could. Where the structure has not been drawn, the methods are vulnerable to the same misframings as the older approaches, and they make the misframings sound more authoritative for being formalised.

## What to ask instead

The chapter closes where the previous one began: with the change in question one is asking. The inherited model asks *what caused this?*, expects a single antecedent, and treats the inquiry as complete when the antecedent has been named. The systems tradition asks four questions, in order, and treats them as the working set.

*What is the outcome whose explanation is being sought?* The question seems trivial. It is not. Most disagreements about why something happened are disagreements, in disguise, about what it is that happened.

*What configuration of producers and contributory conditions, working together, produced it?* This is the structural question. It includes the producer that the inherited model would have named and the conditions the inherited model would have suppressed.

*Which producers and conditions were necessary; which were sufficient given the others; which contributed weight without being either; and which would have changed the outcome if removed?* This is the question Pearl's apparatus, where it can be applied, helps to answer with rigour, and that experienced practitioners answer with disciplined judgement where it cannot.

*Given this account, what would have to change, in the configuration, for the outcome to be different in the direction one wants?* This is the question of intervention, and it is the question for which the producer-product correction was always pointed. It cannot be answered by removing the producer the older model named. It can sometimes be answered by adding a contributory condition the older model overlooked. It is more often answered by a redesign of the configuration so that the structure produces, on its own, what was previously being sought by force.

Singer made the philosophical correction. Ackoff and Emery formalised it. Meadows turned it into a working vocabulary for practitioners. Pearl gave it a rigorous statistical instrument. None of them asked their readers to abandon the analytic tradition. Each asked their readers to recognise that *what caused this?* is the wrong question for situations in which separability is not given. Where separability obtains, the older model is sufficient and the older question stands. Where it does not — and the situations the next part of this book examines are situations in which it does not — the older question, asked in the older way, produces the kind of answer that is correct, vivid, satisfying, and useless.
