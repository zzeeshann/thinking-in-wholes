# Chapter 14 — What 2040 Will Have Learned

This is the chapter the writer would most like to skip.

The book has, until now, engaged with patterns long enough established that arguing about them is arguing with the historical record. Descartes is dead. Ackoff is dead. Meadows, Singer, Whitehead, Bergson, Alexander — all dead, their work finished, their arguments available for any reader patient enough to read them. The writer's task in those chapters has been the relatively safe one of saying what they thought, why it matters, and what one should do with it. The chapters could be wrong about the interpretation; they could not be wrong about the underlying fact, because the underlying fact had already happened.

This chapter is different. It puts predictions on the record. The 2040 reader will know which were right and which were wrong, and how badly. There is no protective historical distance to take cover behind. The bets will be visible.

It is tempting, given how exposed the position is, to write the chapter cautiously — to hedge each prediction with so many qualifications that, whatever happens, it can be claimed the writer foresaw it. This is the standard mode of futurology, and it is one of the reasons futurology is unreadable. It also misses the point. The discipline of prediction is not its accuracy. It is its falsifiability. A prediction that cannot be wrong cannot be useful, because it tells the reader nothing the reader did not already know. Philip Tetlock, whose decades-long study of forecasting is now the standard reference on the subject, found that the forecasters whose predictions were most accurate over time were those who put specific, dated, falsifiable bets on the record — and then, crucially, updated their views on each new piece of evidence.[^1] The unaccurate forecasters, by contrast, were those who used the language of expertise to insulate themselves from being graded.

So the chapter goes on the record. What follows are nine predictions, in three groups of three, attached to dates between 2030 and 2040, written by someone who knows he will be wrong about some of them and who would rather be wrong publicly than appear to have been right by saying nothing.

The first group concerns artificial intelligence — taken, as throughout the book, as a class of technology rather than the products of any particular firm. Two stories about this technology dominate the 2026 conversation. One holds that it will produce an era of abundance that solves most of the practical problems of human life. The other holds that it will produce an era of mass unemployment, concentrated power, and meaning-collapse. The writer's first prediction is that, by 2040, neither story will turn out to have been the right one. The actual story will be a third one that the 2026 conversation barely glimpsed: that the technology's most consequential effects will be psychological and institutional rather than economic. The way human beings make sense of themselves — as workers, as thinkers, as the kind of creatures whose minds matter — will shift more than the goods they produce.

The reasoning behind this prediction is that both of the dominant 2026 stories assume the consequences of artificial intelligence will arrive primarily through the labour market. The abundance story assumes the technology will produce so much more than humans can consume that economic scarcity itself fades. The catastrophe story assumes it will produce so much that humans are no longer needed to produce. Both stories treat the human being as primarily an economic agent whose situation will be determined by what the technology does to that agency. The historical record on technological transitions of comparable scale — the printing press, electricity, the internal combustion engine — suggests that this is not how the most important consequences land. The most important consequences land in what people come to think they are for. The 2040 reader will know this; the 2026 conversation has scarcely begun to.

The second prediction is that the displacement of cognitive labour by 2035 will be at least an order of magnitude larger, in number of jobs, than the displacement of manual labour was over the comparable period at the height of industrial automation. This will catch societies unprepared, because the political institutions that organised the protection of manual labour in the twentieth century — trade unions, labour parties, occupational safety regulations, retraining programmes — were designed for a workforce whose displacement was visible, gradual, and concentrated in a few large industries. Cognitive displacement will be invisible (it happens at desks and screens), rapid (it can be deployed at the speed of software), and dispersed across every sector that involves writing, calculating, analysing, summarising, advising, or routine decision-making. By 2035 the political vocabulary for naming what is happening will not yet exist; by 2040 it will be the central vocabulary of the period.

The third AI prediction is that, by the late 2030s, a class of institutions will have arisen to mediate the relationship between human beings and artificial intelligence systems in ways the 2026 imagination has no model for. These institutions will not be the technology firms themselves; they will be something more like the medical profession or the bar — credentialing bodies, ethical guilds, licensing authorities — whose function is to certify that a particular human being is qualified to direct a particular kind of system in a particular kind of context. The pattern is older than it looks. Every previous technology powerful enough to act in domains of human welfare — pharmaceuticals, surgery, the building of bridges, the arguing of legal cases, the flying of aeroplanes — eventually generated a guild whose function was less to know the technology than to take responsibility for its use. The same pattern will reassert itself, because the same need will. A society that has automated the labour of judgement still needs to know whom to hold accountable when the judgement is wrong, and the only stable answer is a person whose qualification is independently verified. The writer would be unsurprised to find that, by 2040, the most consequential professions on earth are professions that did not exist in 2026.

The second group concerns the climate. The reorganisation of the planet's climate, already visible by 2026 but still routinely framed as a crisis to be averted rather than a condition to be lived in, will be the dominant geopolitical fact of the period covered by this book. The writer's first prediction here is that mitigation, as the dominant frame, will be replaced by adaptation by the early 2030s, not because mitigation will have succeeded but because it will have failed. The political coalitions necessary to reduce emissions at the rate required were not assembled in time. What remains will be the harder and less heroic work of living inside the climate that has been made. By 2040 the dominant question of climate policy will not be how to prevent the change but how to redistribute the consequences.

The second climate prediction is that mass migration driven by climate stress — drought, heat, sea-level rise, the failure of agricultural systems in regions that fed major populations — will be the central political crisis of the 2030s. The institutions that organise national borders, designed for the movement of relatively small numbers of people for relatively orderly reasons, will not be able to manage what arrives. By 2040, the international order will have either developed new mechanisms for accommodating climate migration, or it will have hardened into a more violent and exclusionary form than anything the writer's century had seen since the middle of the twentieth. Which of these comes to pass will be the most important political question between now and then.

The third climate prediction is that, by 2040, the geography of human prosperity will have shifted in ways that 2026 economic forecasting has not begun to take seriously. Several regions currently considered economically marginal — parts of northern Eurasia, parts of inland North America, parts of sub-Saharan Africa with previously unfavourable climates — will have become more habitable, more productive, and more populated. Several regions currently considered economically central will have lost population, capital, and capacity. The maps the 2026 reader takes for granted will look, by 2040, like the maps a 1900 reader took for granted look now: as documents of a previous arrangement.

The third group concerns the institutions and the inequalities they generate. Dan Davies, writing about the way large systems systematically produce outcomes that no participant in them claims to want, named this the unaccountability machine.[^2] His diagnosis follows Stafford Beer's managerial cybernetics, extended into the conditions of the 2020s: large organisations evolve into structures whose decision-making is distributed across so many roles, committees, and processes that no individual decision can be located, no responsibility can be assigned, and no failure can be corrected at the level it occurred. The structures persist not because they work but because no one inside them has the authority or the information necessary to dismantle them. The writer's first prediction is that the unaccountability machine will not be reformed in the period this book is concerned with. It will fail. By the late 2030s, several of the largest institutions of 2026 — corporate, governmental, educational, financial — will have either ceased to exist, fragmented, or been replaced by something organised on different principles. The transitions will be disorderly. The vacuum will be filled by entities that the present has not yet given a name to. Some of those entities will be much better than what they replaced; some will be much worse. The variance will be wide.

The second institutional prediction is that the universities, especially in the English-speaking world, will be reorganised more deeply over the coming fifteen years than over the previous two centuries. A small number of institutions will retain their function as credentialing factories for the elite, much as they have done for several centuries. The great majority of others will lose their financial model, in part because the value of generic credentials will collapse against the rising value of demonstrated capability, and in part because the cognitive work the universities trained for will have been substantially automated. The institutions that survive in serviceable form will be the ones that have repositioned around what cannot be automated: the formation of judgement, the training of attention, the practice of being with other minds in real time.

The third institutional prediction is that the household — the small unit of two or three or four people sharing a roof and an economic life — will reassert itself, by the late 2030s, as a unit of production rather than only of consumption. This is a return to a much older arrangement. The wage-earning, consumption-oriented household of the late twentieth century was a historical anomaly built on conditions (cheap energy, mass employment, geographic stability) that are no longer holding. The household of 2040 will be more economically mixed, more multi-generational, and more involved in producing some fraction of what it needs. This is not a romantic prediction. It is a structural one. When the wage cannot be relied on, the household has to do more.

These nine predictions are made in the knowledge that several will turn out wrong. The writer would be very surprised to be right about more than five or six. He would be unsurprised to be wrong about three or four. The prediction he would most wish to revisit, if he could speak to himself in 2040, is the third climate prediction; the geography of prosperity is the kind of thing one is almost certainly under-imagining. The prediction he would most wish to be wrong about is the second one in the climate group; mass migration is the kind of crisis from which institutions either learn or harden, and the cost of the second outcome is too high to want.

There are things, finally, the writer hopes he is wrong about. He hopes he is wrong about the political response to climate stress — that it will be more cooperative and less authoritarian than the trajectory of the 2020s suggests. He hopes he is wrong about the deepening of inequality during the AI transition; it is structurally likely, but it is not inevitable, and the institutions that reduce its likelihood are knowable and could be built. He hopes he is wrong about the loneliness — that, by 2040, the slow disorganisation of the social bonds that keep human beings recognisably human will have been arrested, and a generation will have grown up better connected to each other than the one that wrote this book.

He hopes he is wrong about the universities — that some among them will turn out to have done the harder work of repositioning around what cannot be automated, and will produce graduates whose formation, by 2040, looks not less but more humanely educated than the formation of their grandparents. He hopes he is wrong about the international order — that the period now beginning will turn out to have been, on the longer view, a transition from one cooperative arrangement to another, rather than the descent into the fragmented arrangement the early 2030s seem to be sketching.

The hopes are not predictions. They are bets the writer would gladly lose. He is making them on the record so that, if they come to pass, the people responsible for the work will have been correctly identified as having done something the 2026 view would not have predicted them to do. Hope, treated rigorously, is a form of attention to what is possible but not yet probable. It is closer to the kind of bet a careful investor makes than to the kind of wish a careless person makes. The writer would not write down a hope he could not, on a serious accounting, give some chance of becoming true.

What remains is the closing argument. The book has tried, across thirteen previous chapters, to describe a way of thinking — old, repeatedly developed, repeatedly underused — that the world has not yet learned to apply at the scale of its actual problems. The argument has been negative in places: parts-thinking was the operating principle of the modern world, and it has reached the limits of its competence. The argument has also been positive: synthesis is real, the systems tradition is rich, and the practice of holding the whole in mind is something a person can learn.

The question the closing chapter has to answer is what unit the systems response runs on. The natural temptations are to name a technology, an institution, or a policy. The writer would resist all three. There is no technology that can produce systems thinking in a person who has not chosen to learn it. There is no institution whose structure is sufficient to do the work, because institutions are structurally Cartesian and the work in question is structurally synthetic. There is no policy that can require its citizens to attend to the wholes of their own lives, because the practice cannot be enforced; it can only be cultivated. The systems response runs on persons. One at a time.

Russell Ackoff, late in his career, drew a distinction between growth and development that this book has returned to several times.[^3] Growth is more of what one already has. Development is more of what one is capable of becoming. Most of what the modern world calls progress is growth in Ackoff's sense — more wealth, more output, more reach, more options. Development is rarer and harder to measure. It cannot be summed across populations the way growth can. It happens one person at a time, in the slow and unobserved work of becoming the kind of human being who can hold a whole in mind.

A growing organisation, an Ackoff student once observed, is one that gets bigger; a developing organisation is one that gets better at what it has chosen to be. The same is true of a person. A growing person accumulates — credentials, possessions, relationships measured by quantity, experiences treated as a portfolio. A developing person becomes — more attentive to what is in front of them, more honest about what they do not understand, more capable of distinguishing what matters from what merely interrupts. Growth can be sustained by a culture that delivers consumption to the surface of a life. Development cannot; it requires the kind of inwardness that consumption interferes with. This is one reason the practice of development has been hard to scale even when it has been recognised.

The developed person, in Ackoff's sense, is the unit the systems response actually requires. Not the consumer; consumers cannot resist a system whose structure rewards consumption. Not the voter; voters cannot legislate against a system whose structure is faster than legislation. Not even the citizen, in the political sense, because political identity is not the level at which systems thinking is held. It is held in the person — in the formed habits of attention, in the capacity to recognise a connection that the prevailing vocabulary obscures, in the willingness to act on the recognition even when no one in the person's institution is rewarded for doing so.

This is the book's central bet. Whatever institutions of 2040 turn out to address the systemic problems of the period — and the writer believes some will — those institutions will not have produced themselves. They will have been built by people whose own development equipped them to see what their period needed and to work toward it. The unit of the response is the developed life. Everything else follows from that.

Stewart Brand, in *The Clock of the Long Now*, observed that civilisations capable of thinking on long time-scales are civilisations capable of acting on long ones.[^4] The reverse is also true. Civilisations whose attention spans contract to the next quarter cannot build the institutions a long crisis requires. The recovery of attention, at the level of a person and at the level of a culture, is not an aesthetic or therapeutic concern. It is the precondition for everything else.

The writer of this book is in 2026, in the position of someone who has stood in a long-running storm and tried to describe its weather. The reader of this book is in 2040, in the position of someone who knows how the storm continued, and what it produced, and what it left standing. Between them is the work this book is about. The 2040 reader will know, far better than the writer, which of the predictions in this chapter were closest to right and which were embarrassingly off; which of the institutions named were correctly identified as failing and which proved more durable than expected; which of the hopes were honoured and which were not.

What the writer can offer, from inside the storm, is a description of the discipline that — if practised by enough people, in enough places, in time — would have made the difference. The discipline is the recovery of synthesis. The unit is the developed person. The practice is the patient and unfashionable work of holding the whole in mind while doing the necessary, undignified labour of attending to its parts.

If, by 2040, this discipline has come into wider practice than the trajectory of 2026 suggested it would, the book will have been useful. If it has not, the book will at least have been one record of what was being thought, in those years, by someone who saw the problem and tried to name the response. Either way, the work continues. It always did.
