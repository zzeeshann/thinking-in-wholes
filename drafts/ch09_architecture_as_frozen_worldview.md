# Chapter 9 — Architecture as Frozen Worldview

A street, in any city more than fifty years old, has been used as something its designers did not anticipate. The blocks were laid out for one kind of commerce and now hold another. The setbacks were drawn for one kind of street life and now allow a different one. The proportions of the lots were sized for one model of family and now house several. What the street is, in any week of its existence, is not what the street was designed for. What the street is is whatever its designers' framework happens to permit, given the lives the inhabitants are trying to live inside it.

Whether the street works depends, more than on anything else, on whether the original framework was generous or narrow. A street designed for one specific use and incapable of any other is a street that has, by the time the use changed, already failed. A street whose original design left room for changes the designers could not foresee is a street that has, fifty years on, become more itself rather than less. The same is true of a building. The same is true of a neighbourhood. The same is true, at the largest scale, of an entire city.

Buildings and cities are ossified ideas. What an architect or a planner believed about how life is lived, what a building is for, what a city should look like — these beliefs get poured into form, and the form outlasts the belief by decades or centuries. A building constructed a century ago still tells its present inhabitants what its architect believed about light, work, family, and the street. The architect is dead; the belief is still telling the inhabitants where to put the table.

Form, in the built environment, is not a neutral container for life. Form is a frozen claim about what life is. A city full of single-family houses on quarter-acre lots, accessible only by car, is a city built on the claim that the family is a nuclear unit, the workplace is elsewhere, the street is a corridor for transit, and the public realm is something one drives between. A city built around a market square that one walks to from one's flat above the workshop is a city built on a different claim, just as much a claim, just as much frozen into the form. Neither city neutrally accommodates life. Each city instructs its inhabitants in how to live, by being the only structure in which their living can occur.

This is why the architect and the planner are systems thinkers whether they want to be or not. What they put into the form will determine, for as long as the form lasts, what the people inside the form can do. The systems tradition has names for this — it speaks of the *configuration* that admits some flows and excludes others, the *boundary conditions* that constrain the possibility space — but the architect already knew. The architect's vocabulary is *programme*, *plan*, *circulation*, *type*. The vocabulary is older than the systems tradition's vocabulary. What the systems tradition can offer the architect is not new ideas about form; it is a vocabulary for noticing what was always true about form, and for assessing whether a given form has been good or bad to the lives that have had to live inside it.

## What Alexander saw

Christopher Alexander's central observation was that the cultures whose buildings have been beautiful for a long time — the medieval town, the Japanese village, the Anatolian house, the Cotswold cottage — did not have professional architects. The buildings were built by the people who would live in them, or by builders working in close conversation with those people, using a body of knowledge passed down by example. Each generation modified and extended what the previous generation had built. No single mind designed any one building from scratch; every building was the local resolution of a long tradition of decisions about how to put a wall up, where to put a window, how to relate the house to the street.

What Alexander noticed was that these traditions, when one looked carefully at them, were not random. They contained a recurring set of arrangements — a window seat that catches the morning light, a porch that is half inside and half out, a courtyard that the rooms open onto, a path that arrives at the front door at an angle that lets the visitor see the threshold before they reach it. These arrangements appeared in cultures that had no contact with each other. They appeared because they fit the lives the buildings had to hold. He gave them a name. He called them *patterns*.

A pattern, on Alexander's account, was a recurring problem in a context together with the recurring solution that had been found, across many cultures and many centuries, to resolve it. The pattern *Light on Two Sides of Every Room* was the recurring solution to the problem that a room with light from only one side felt narrow and oppressive in a way it took the inhabitants weeks to identify. The pattern *Entrance Transition* was the recurring solution to the problem that a door opening directly from the street to the interior left the inhabitant no moment in which to leave the street behind. The patterns were not aesthetic preferences. They were the residue of millions of small experiments in living, retained because they fit how human bodies and human attention actually behaved.[^1]

The book Alexander and his collaborators wrote on this — a long catalogue of two hundred and fifty-three patterns, ranging from regional patterns at the largest scale down to the smallest interior arrangements — became a reference work that has stayed in print for nearly half a century.[^2] What the book offered was something the systems tradition had been looking for from another direction: a vocabulary in which the recurring fits between a whole and its parts could be named, taught, and applied without being reduced to either rule or whim.

His larger claim went further. In *The Timeless Way of Building*, the book that preceded *A Pattern Language* and explained its philosophy, Alexander argued that the question of whether a building is good or bad is not a question of taste. It is a question of whether the building is alive — whether the configuration of its parts allows the life of its inhabitants to be itself, or whether the configuration constrains and deforms that life. He gave the property a name, although he was clear that the name was provisional, since what he was naming had no single English word. He called it *the quality without a name*.[^3] A room has it or does not. A street has it or does not. A city has it or does not. The quality is recognisable to the inhabitant — the inhabitant knows when a room is alive and when it is not — but it cannot be analysed into a set of measurable features, because the quality is the configuration, not any feature of the configuration.

Alexander's later work, particularly the four-volume *The Nature of Order*, extended this claim into a broader one about wholeness as a property of physical configurations.[^4] The argument is contested. What is not contested is the smaller observation underneath it: that the test of a building is not its appearance in a photograph but its effect on the lives of the people who have to live inside it, and that this test cannot be passed by optimising any single feature. The building either holds the life or fails to. Holding the life is the whole; the parts of the building succeed only insofar as they contribute to the holding.

## What Jacobs saw

Jane Jacobs, working at the same period as Alexander but on the scale of cities rather than buildings, came at the same problem from the other end. Her observation was that planners and architects, when they attempted to design cities, almost always made the cities worse. The cities that worked — the lively, dense, complicated districts where strangers walked safely after dark, where new ideas were tried and small businesses survived their first year, where children played in sight of adults who knew them — were the cities that had grown without a plan, or in spite of one. The cities that failed — the empty plazas, the dangerous projects, the dead boulevards — were almost always the cities that had been planned hardest.

Her diagnosis, in *The Death and Life of Great American Cities*, was that the planning tradition had treated the city as the wrong kind of system. It had treated the city as a problem of independent variables — units of housing per parcel here, traffic flow per hour there, population per acre somewhere else — to be optimised one at a time, when the city was actually a problem of what Warren Weaver had called *organised complexity*, the kind of system in which a moderate number of variables interact in ways that cannot be decomposed without losing what one was trying to study.[^5] The planner who optimised population density without attending to the configuration of uses on the street was producing a tower in a park; the tower in a park was empty of life because the configuration that produced street life had been abolished in the optimisation.

Her positive account of what made a district work was a description of four conditions, each of which had to hold for the others to do their work. The district had to combine more than one primary use, so that the streets were occupied by different kinds of people at different times of day. The blocks had to be short, so that the network of possible paths through the district was dense and pedestrians could choose their way. The buildings had to be of varying ages and conditions, so that the rents varied and a range of enterprises could afford to be there. The density of inhabitants had to be sufficient to support the small-scale commerce and the casual surveillance that made the streets safe. The four conditions were not a recipe; they were the conditions a district had to meet in order for its complexity to organise itself. Where they were met, the district came alive. Where any one was missing, the others could not compensate for it.

Jacobs's later book, *The Economy of Cities*, took the argument to a larger scale.[^6] Cities were not, on her account, secondary derivatives of an agricultural economy that had produced surplus to support them. Cities were the original engine of economic development; the agricultural economies of their hinterlands were derivatives of the cities, not the other way around. The capacity of a city to generate new work — new kinds of work that had not existed before, by improvisation on the work that already existed there — depended on the same conditions that made its districts come alive. A city of single-purpose districts, however efficient at any one purpose, was a city without the configuration in which novelty could be improvised.

The temptation, in summarising Jacobs, is to take her four conditions as a checklist a planner might apply. This is the wrong reading. The four conditions describe the configuration in which a city can be alive; the conditions cannot be applied as separate decisions, because each one depends on the others to do its work. A district with mixed primary uses but blocks too long is not improved by the mixing. A district with short blocks but a single primary use is not improved by the brevity. The configuration is the whole; the four conditions are the names of the relationships that have to be present in the whole for the whole to function. To plan for any one in isolation is to repeat the error that produced the tower in the park.

## The Metabolist proposal

There was, in mid-twentieth-century Japan, a movement of architects who took the question further. They had grown up in a country whose cities had been destroyed by aerial bombardment within their living memory; they had watched the cities be rebuilt, in ways many of them found dispiriting, by the international modernist conventions of the period. They proposed an alternative. The alternative was that buildings should be designed as biological systems whose parts could be replaced and renewed while the building itself continued. A tower would consist of a permanent core — the structural spine and the service shafts — and a set of removable units that plugged into it. As the units aged, they would be unplugged and replaced. As the family that inhabited a unit grew or shrank or changed its work, the unit itself would be swapped for a different unit. The building's identity would be in its core; its substance would be a flow.

The proposal, on the page, was striking. Most of it remained on the page. A few of the buildings the movement actually built were demolished within decades, sometimes by neglect, sometimes because the units that were supposed to be replaceable had never in fact been replaced; the architecture had been designed for change but the institutions around the architecture had not been. The movement's significance, in retrospect, is not its building record. Its significance is that it took seriously a question most of its contemporaries did not — the question of what it would mean to design a building for the lives that would actually pass through it, rather than for the form the building was supposed to keep. The question, asked in that form, has not been adequately answered since.

The Metabolist answer assumed that a building was a system whose parts had different rates of change, and that the design's job was to honour those different rates rather than to fix them all to the rate of the slowest. This is a systems claim, made architecturally. The claim has been hard to act on, in part because the institutions that finance and regulate buildings treat the building as a fixed object, and in part because the architectural profession's reward structures favour the design of the fixed object rather than the design for change. The proposal stands as a challenge to which the built environment has not yet figured out how to respond.

## Brand on how buildings learn

The most useful contemporary treatment of the same observation is Stewart Brand's, in a book called *How Buildings Learn*. Brand's argument was that buildings, after they are built, are altered by the people who live in them, and that the alteration is the building's life. A building that is altered well is a building that has learned to fit its inhabitants. A building that resists alteration, or that is altered badly, is a building that fights its inhabitants. The architect's choice, on Brand's account, is not whether the building will change but whether the building will change well.[^7]

He gave the analysis a structural form. A building, he argued, consists of layers that change at different rates: the *site* changes over centuries; the *structure* over many decades; the *skin* — the cladding, the windows — over decades; the *services* — wiring, plumbing, ducts — over a span shorter still; the *space plan* — the partitioning of the interior — over years; the *stuff* — the furniture, the objects — over weeks or days. A building designed so that the fast-changing layers can move without disturbing the slow-changing ones is a building that grows old gracefully. A building designed so that the layers are entangled — services run through the structure, the space plan welded to the skin — is a building that becomes harder to inhabit with every year that passes, because every alteration the inhabitants need to make costs more than the alteration is worth.

Brand's analysis is the Metabolist question answered at the level of the existing building stock. One does not need to have designed the building from scratch as a Metabolist building in order to apply the analysis. One needs only to ask, of any existing building, what its layers are, how its layers are entangled or separated, and what the building's life over the next fifty years will require of those layers. The answers are usually instructive. Most of the buildings produced by the architectural profession in the second half of the twentieth century, on Brand's evidence, were buildings that had been designed against learning. They were buildings whose layers had been welded together because the architect had wanted to control the building's appearance over time, and the inhabitants had paid for the welding in inconvenience and expense for as long as the buildings stood.

## Why cities fail

The pattern that connects Alexander, Jacobs, the Metabolists, and Brand is a pattern about what gets optimised in the design of a built environment, and what gets neglected. In each case, the failed design has been an optimisation of one variable — the planned use, the visual appearance, the projected traffic flow, the per-square-metre yield — at the expense of the configuration the inhabitants are trying to live inside. The successful design has been one that has refused to optimise the single variable and has held out for the configuration.

The reason single-variable optimisation is the easier path is the reason this book has catalogued already. The single variable is measurable. The configuration is not. The single variable can be assigned to a professional whose competence is in maximising that variable, and the maximisation can be presented to the client as a result. The configuration cannot be assigned in the same way, because the configuration is what holds when many things are well-balanced against each other, and no single professional's competence is sufficient to hold the balance. The competence required for the configuration is the competence to refuse to optimise, even when the refusal looks, from outside, like a failure to do the job.

The cities that have failed their inhabitants over the last century have failed for that reason. They were planned for the variable the planner was trained to maximise. The variable was not, in any of the failed cases, the inhabitants' lives. It was traffic throughput, or population density, or development yield, or the visual coherence of the modernist plan. The variable was maximised. The lives were not.

The cities that have continued to work have, very often, been the cities the planner did not reach. They have been the older quarters, the unfashionable districts, the buildings that survived because no one thought them important enough to clear. Their working has had nothing to do with their being old; it has had everything to do with their having been built in periods, or in places, where no professional had the authority to optimise a single variable, and the inhabitants therefore had room to make the configuration.

## Designing for the whole life

What it would mean to design for the whole life, rather than for the planned function, is what the rest of the systems tradition implies for those whose work is the built environment. It would mean asking, of any proposal, what configuration of activities it would admit and what configuration it would exclude. It would mean accepting that the configuration cannot be specified in advance, because the inhabitants will be living their lives, not the architect's image of their lives, and the configuration will have to permit lives the architect did not foresee. It would mean designing for change as the normal case, not as the disturbance to be resisted. It would mean treating the inhabitants' alteration of the building, over the decades the building will stand, as the building's real life, and the architect's drawings as the building's first proposal — a proposal the inhabitants will revise.

The professional culture of the built environment makes this hard, for reasons the systems tradition has named clearly elsewhere. The professional is rewarded for what is visible in the photograph at the moment of completion; the inhabitants' decades of revision are invisible in the professional's portfolio, and worse, they often look like damage. The client is the one who pays for the construction; the inhabitants who will pay for the life of the building over its long use are usually not in the room when the design is decided. The institutions that finance and regulate the building treat it as a fixed asset whose value depends on its remaining as built. Each of these arrangements rewards the design of the object and punishes the design for the life. The architect who wants to design for the life is fighting the institutions in which the architect's work has to occur.

The chapter does not pretend the fight is easy. It claims only that the discipline the systems tradition asks of the maker — the refusal to optimise the single variable, the obligation to design for the configuration the part has to fit, the respect for the slow rates of change against the temptations of the fast — is the same discipline a designer of buildings or cities most needs and most rarely meets. The buildings and cities that have endured, that have continued to hold the lives that have passed through them, are the ones whose original designers held that discipline, by training or by accident or because the institutional pressures of their place and time happened to permit it. The buildings and cities that have failed have failed the same way. The lesson of the failure is the lesson of the rest of this book, frozen into walls.
