# Chapter 3 — The Anatomy of a System

A surgeon removes an appendix. The patient lives, recovers, walks out of the hospital. The appendix sits in a steel tray. Asked what the appendix is for, the surgeon shrugs — vestigial, mostly; a refuge for some gut bacteria, perhaps; nothing essential. Asked what the appendix *was* for, the same surgeon would still shrug. The question is malformed. The appendix is not for anything in isolation. Its membership in the body is what gave it whatever role it had, and removing it from the body did not reveal that role; it only removed the conditions under which the role could appear.

This is not a mystery. It is the basic situation of any part within a whole. The part has properties; the whole has different properties; and the relation between them is not subtraction. Yet the whole of inherited language about systems pretends otherwise. We say a thing *consists of* its parts. We say it can be *broken down* into its components. We say we *understand* it once we have the inventory. None of these phrases is wrong, exactly. They are simply written for a different kind of object — one whose behaviour reduces to the behaviour of its parts, taken one at a time. For systems, they are misleading at exactly the point where understanding is most needed.

This chapter sets out a working vocabulary for systems. Not a glossary — a glossary would suggest the words can be looked up and put down again. The terms here are tools for seeing. Once a person can see emergence, or a feedback loop, or a delay, the seeing does not retract when the page is turned. The world acquires a new texture, and the texture stays. The aim of the chapter is to make seven such textures available, each anchored in an example that survives translation across domains.

The seven are emergence, feedback (reinforcing and balancing), delay, hierarchy, boundary, purpose, and resilience. They are not all the words a systems thinker uses, but they are the words the rest of this book leans on. Later chapters will return to them.

## Emergence

Water is wet. Two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom, taken separately, are not wet — wetness is not a property of either element. Water is also liquid at room temperature, which neither hydrogen nor oxygen is. It freezes into a solid that floats, against the general behaviour of solids in liquids of their own kind, and this anomaly is the reason fish survive winter. None of these properties are deducible from the chemistry of the constituents in the way the mass of a chair is deducible from the mass of its legs and seat. They emerge when the constituents enter into a particular kind of relation.

This is what *emergence* names: a property of the whole that is not a property of any part, and that arises from the relations among the parts.[^1] Emergence is the reason analytic methods — the disassembly into parts that the previous chapter described as the Cartesian inheritance — fail when applied to systems. The disassembly does not preserve what is being studied. It produces a different object: a heap.

Emergence is not magic, despite the discomfort of those trained to expect every property to be derivable. The cause of emergent behaviour is the configuration. A flock of starlings, turning together over an evening field, is not a centrally directed object; each bird is following a small set of local rules — match heading with neighbours, keep distance from neighbours, avoid predator. Out of those local rules, at scale, comes the synchronised flight that looks intelligent and is. The intelligence belongs to the flock, not to any bird.[^2] Take any individual bird out of the flock and the property disappears, exactly as the appendix's function disappears when the appendix is removed.

The implication for any practitioner — designer, engineer, writer, builder — is direct. If the property you care about is emergent, you cannot install it by working on the parts in isolation. You can only set the conditions under which it has a chance to arise. The intelligence of an organisation, the coherence of a city, the warmth of a meal, the wholeness of a book — each is emergent. None can be assembled.

For the maker, this is the most useful idea in the chapter's vocabulary, and the one most easily lost in the long hours of working on parts. The rightness of a building is not a property of any brick or window. The unity of a piece of music is not a property of any chord. The voice of a book is not in the sentence; it is what the sentences, in succession, do to the reader's attention. A maker who works only on parts produces a heap of well-finished parts. A maker who has internalised the concept of emergence still has to finish each part — the sentence still has to land, the chord still has to sound — but knows, while doing so, what the parts are for. They are what the whole is built out of, and the whole is what the work is.

## Feedback

A thermostat measures the temperature of a room. If the room is colder than the setpoint, the thermostat closes a circuit, the furnace runs, the room warms. Once the room reaches the setpoint, the circuit opens, the furnace cuts off, the room begins to cool — at which point the thermostat re-engages, and the cycle resumes. The whole arrangement holds the room within a narrow band of temperatures around the setpoint. This is the simplest example of *feedback*: a measurement of the system's state is fed back into the system as input, modifying its behaviour.[^3]

Feedback comes in two forms, and the difference between them is the difference between the most common dynamic patterns in human life. A *balancing* loop, like the thermostat, pushes the system toward a target. The wider the gap between current state and target, the harder the loop pushes back. Balancing loops are stabilising — when they work, they make a system seek and hold a steady state. Hunger and satiation form a balancing loop. Body temperature, blood sugar, the price discovery of a market in equilibrium, the steering of a car along a lane — all balancing loops. Where there is regulation, there is a balancing loop somewhere in the structure.

A *reinforcing* loop runs in the other direction. The output amplifies the input. Compound interest is reinforcing — the larger the principal, the larger the interest, which becomes the larger principal. So is panic in a crowd: a few running people prompt more to run, who prompt more still. So is the lonely spiral of a person who, isolated for long enough, loses the social skills that would let them re-enter company; and so is the contrary loop in a flourishing relationship, in which trust earned earlier makes the next risk easier and the next bond firmer. Reinforcing loops are the engines of growth, of decline, of habit, of compound effects of every kind. They explain why so much that matters does not behave linearly. They explain why a small initial advantage often becomes a large later one — and why a small initial neglect, allowed to run, often becomes catastrophic.

The crucial point is that almost every interesting system is a tangle of loops. The challenge is not whether feedback is present but which loops dominate, in which conditions, on which timescales. A reinforcing loop running unchecked is what people mean when they speak of a vicious or virtuous circle. A balancing loop unable to keep up is what they mean when they speak of a system breaking its own regulator. The classical case study of cybernetic engineering is the regulation of complex machinery; the same logic, found again in living organisms and human institutions, is what makes feedback the most generally useful concept in the systems-thinking vocabulary.[^4]

The serious analyst gets past intuition by drawing the structure. The mid-twentieth-century engineering tradition that came to be called system dynamics took feedback structures and made them formal — diagrams of stocks (the accumulations a system holds), flows (the rates at which the stocks fill or drain), and the loops that connect them.[^5] The point of that formalism is not to reach a number. The point is that once a structure is drawn, the dominant loops can be seen, the likely behaviour over time can be reasoned about, and the points where intervention will have outsized effect become visible. A practitioner who can sketch a feedback diagram of a problem has done most of the conceptual work; what remains is largely refinement.

## Delay

Most systems do not respond instantly. The signal arrives; the change begins; the result appears later. This gap between cause and effect is the *delay*, and it is one of the chapter's plain words for one of the most powerful and most consistently underestimated features of real systems.

A shower with old plumbing teaches the lesson cheaply. The bather turns the cold tap; the water is still scalding because the hot already in the line has not yet drained; the bather, finding nothing changed, turns the cold further; the cooler mix arrives a moment later, now far too cold; the bather over-corrects in the other direction; and so on, oscillating between extremes, never settling, until either the bather learns to wait between adjustments or someone replaces the plumbing. The bather's mistake is not stupidity. It is the very natural one of assuming that the response will arrive in the same moment as the cause. With a delay in the loop, that assumption produces overshoot, undershoot, and oscillation — sometimes for a long time, sometimes forever.

The bather's shower is the model for many of the patterns this book later examines. The economy that overheats while the central bank waits for last quarter's data. The forest that has been logged at sustainable rates by the previous generation's measure but not by the current generation's growth. The relationship in which an act of repair, made too late, fails to register, and is then abandoned just before it would have worked. The training programme cancelled at the moment when a workforce trained five years earlier finally would have paid back the cost of training them. The drug that is cleared from the bloodstream in hours but is still doing its work weeks later in tissues. Delays come in many forms — material delays, perception delays, decision delays, response delays — and a system can have several delays compounded along a single chain of cause.

Where a delay sits inside a balancing loop, the loop will overshoot its target before it reaches it, and oscillate around it. Where a delay sits inside a reinforcing loop, the spiral will run further than its driver can see before its full extent appears. The longer the delay, the harder the system is to steer; and the more confident the steerer, the worse the over-correction. Delays make patience a technical, not a sentimental, virtue. The chapter on overshoot and the grief of premature action returns to this point.

## Hierarchy

The body has cells. Cells are arranged into tissues. Tissues compose organs. Organs work together as systems — circulatory, respiratory, nervous — which compose the body, which lives within a family, which lives within a community, and so on outward to scales the body itself never directly senses. At each level the next-lower components are themselves whole systems, with their own emergent properties and their own internal regulation. At each level the components together form a system whose properties are not those of the level below.

This is *hierarchy*: the nesting of systems within systems.[^6] The word has been damaged by its use to mean ranks of authority, but the technical meaning is older and broader. It refers to a structural fact about complex systems — that they are layered, with each layer more or less self-contained, each communicating with adjacent layers through restricted interfaces. The layers are real; they are not arbitrary impositions of a tidy mind. They are how complexity manages itself. A cell that had to coordinate every protein interaction with every other cell in the body would seize. The layered structure is what makes coordination possible at scale.

The practical consequence is that the right level of analysis for any question is rarely obvious. Why is this organism unhealthy? The answer might lie in a cell, or in an organ, or in a relationship, or in a workplace, or in the air of the city. Each level operates by its own logic. Each is necessary; none is sufficient. The systems-thinking habit is to ask, of any phenomenon, *at what level does this become legible?* — and to be willing to find that the level is not the one being studied. A symptom in the body may be a problem in the family. A failure in a department may be a problem in the company's purpose. A breakdown in a city may be a problem in the watershed.

Hierarchy also explains why systems can fail in ways that are hard to assign blame for. A higher-level pattern can persist when no individual at the lower level is doing anything wrong. The traffic jam is the famous case. No driver is at fault; each is making a sensible local decision; the jam is the whole's response to a configuration the parts cannot, on their own, change.

## Boundary

A cell has a membrane. The membrane is real, physical, semi-permeable; it controls what enters and leaves, and the cell's life depends on that control. So far the boundary is in the world. But where does the cell end and the body begin? The membrane is the obvious answer; yet the cell is functionally inseparable from its blood supply, its nervous signals, its bath of hormones, its ambient temperature. To draw the boundary at the membrane is a choice that serves some questions and obscures others.

Every system has a *boundary*: a line between what is inside it and what is outside. It is where the system meets its environment, and where the analyst meets the modeller's first hard choice — because the boundary is, almost always, partly a property of the system and partly a choice of the person studying it.[^7]

The same is true of every system worth studying. The boundary of a family is unclear: by kinship? By household? By the people one would die for? The boundary of a city is unclear: legal limits, watershed, commute zone, supply chain? The boundary of a person is unclear in ways that are not merely poetic: the gut bacteria carry out functions a body could not perform without them; the language a person thinks in was given by a community; the body's sense of where it is relies on the floor, the gravity, the air. Drawing a boundary too narrowly omits causes. Drawing it too widely renders the system unstudiable.

The systems thinker's discipline here is honesty. The boundary of an analysis is to be named, not assumed. *Within these limits, this is what we can say. Outside these limits, the system continues, and our model becomes less reliable.* This is not failure. It is competence. A practitioner who claims a boundary they have not drawn is fooling themselves; a practitioner who claims a model that holds beyond the boundary is fooling others.

## Purpose

A system's purpose is not what its designers say it is. It is not what its workers wish it were. It is what the system, taken as a whole, actually does. A school whose stated purpose is education but whose dominant behaviour is the production of credentials and the sorting of children by social class has, as its actual purpose, credentialing and sorting. A hospital whose stated purpose is healing but whose dominant behaviour is the management of insurance claims has, as its actual purpose, claims management. To object that this is unfair to the school's teachers or the hospital's doctors is to misunderstand the diagnostic. Purpose at the system level is independent of intention at any individual level. It is what the system, on average, in the long run, can be relied upon to produce.[^8]

When a person inside a system that is doing harm protests that they did not intend the harm, the protest is, at the level of the individual, often correct. They did not intend it. At the level of the system, the protest is irrelevant. The harm is the purpose; the system is doing what it does. To change what it does, the system has to be redesigned, and redesign begins with the diagnosis the actual purpose forces.[^9]

A practitioner who wishes to understand a system must therefore look past what is said about it, including what they themselves say, and read the purpose off the behaviour. Where the behaviour and the stated purpose diverge, the behaviour is the truth.

The diagnostic has a constructive sequel. Once the actual purpose of a system is named, the question of what the system *should* be doing becomes answerable, because there is now something concrete to compare it against. The discipline of organisational design — Ackoff's life's work, and the inheritance Chapter 2 traced — proceeds from this point. A redesign that leaves the actual purpose unexamined is cosmetic. A redesign that begins by writing down what the system, as it stands, in fact produces — and then asking whether anyone would build a system to produce that — is the only kind that has a chance of changing what the system does.

## Resilience

A forest burns; given a season, it returns. A body cuts itself; given time, it heals. An institution loses its leader; given succession, it continues. A language loses speakers; given a community, it survives. In each case the system has been pushed away from its functioning state, and in each case it has the capacity to find its way back. That capacity is *resilience*: the ability of a system to maintain its function under shock, perturbation, or stress. It is a property of the system as a whole — of its redundancies, its slack, its diverse pathways for getting things done, its willingness to lose efficiency in the short term in exchange for survival in the long term.

Resilience is often confused with strength, but it is closer to the opposite. A strong thing resists shock by not bending. A resilient thing absorbs the shock by bending and then recovering. A bridge built strong but not resilient will hold every load until the load that breaks it; a bridge built resilient will sway through the wind that would have shattered the strong one, and stand the next morning. The same logic obtains in living things. The body that has never been ill is the body least equipped to handle illness. The institution that has never failed is the institution most likely to fail catastrophically. Resilience is built by exposure to manageable failure, not by the absence of failure.

The hostility between resilience and a particular kind of efficiency is one of the chapter's hardest practical lessons. To build resilience is to maintain redundancies that, in any given quarter, look wasteful. The second pathway to the same outcome. The relationship that pays no immediate dividend. The skill that has not yet been needed. Optimised systems shed these as inefficiencies; resilient systems carry them as insurance. When the shock arrives — and shocks arrive — the optimised system, having no give, breaks at exactly the point optimisation taught it not to reinforce. The resilient system, having absorbed many small shocks already, takes the large one as one more.

This is why resilience cannot be added to a system at the moment it is needed. It is a structural property, accumulated over time, often invisible until it is tested. Building it is a discipline of the long view. Stripping it for short-term efficiency is a temptation almost every system, given enough quarters of pressure, will eventually accept. The ones that do are the ones that fail, and the failure is, almost always, sudden, surprising to the people inside, and obvious in retrospect to the people outside.

## What anatomy is for

Seven words. Each names a feature any practitioner can be trained to see. Naming them is not the work; it is the floor on which the work stands. The work begins when the words become operative — when a person, looking at a stalled organisation, sees not a list of complaints but a tangle of feedback loops with mismatched delays, a hierarchy whose levels have been confused, a boundary drawn too narrowly, a purpose drifting from its statement, a resilience eroding under quarterly optimisation.

This vocabulary did not arrive in one piece. The mid-twentieth century saw a converging effort — among biologists, mathematicians, engineers, ecologists, organisational thinkers — to find words for what every honest practitioner had always known: that the world they were studying was not a heap. The general system theory of the biologist who wished to write an account of life that did not depend on any particular substrate;[^10] the cybernetic engineering that found in feedback the same logic in machines, in animals, and in social systems;[^11] the system dynamics of the engineer who built simulation models of feedback structures in industrial economies;[^12] the late-century work on emergence in complex adaptive systems;[^13] and the systems-thinking primers that translated the technical vocabulary into a working idiom — these are not separate fields. They are facets of one inquiry, conducted under different lighting.

What follows in this book takes the vocabulary as given and uses it. The pathologies of the next part — the tyranny of local optima, the blindness inside a loop, the grief of premature action — are described in this language because no other language describes them as well. The domains of the third part — making, building, engineering, philosophising — are examined through this lens because each comes alive when seen this way. The practice of the fourth part — the connected life, the developed person — rests on the assumption that a person is a system, embedded in larger systems, governed by these dynamics whether or not the person knows.

A reader who has stayed with this chapter has the words. The remainder of the book is what they make visible.
