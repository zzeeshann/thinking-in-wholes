# Chapter 10 — Engineering Beyond Mechanism

An engineer is at a desk, designing a part. The part has specifications. It must hold a particular load. It must withstand a particular temperature for a particular duration. It must endure a particular number of cycles before its failure rate climbs to a level the design will not tolerate. Each specification has been written by an engineer earlier in the design chain, who was working from specifications written further upstream by another engineer, who was working from a project requirement signed by a manager, who was working from a contract the company had signed with another company. The chain is long. The engineer at this desk has the last specification in the chain. Her task is to satisfy it.

Her training has equipped her for this. The materials behave in known ways. The loads can be calculated. The cycle counts can be tested. When she has finished, the specification will be met. The part will leave her desk and go to manufacturing, where it will be produced, and from manufacturing to assembly, where it will be combined with hundreds of other parts whose engineers have done the same kind of work at their own desks. The hundreds of parts will become a machine. The machine will be installed in a system. The system will be embedded in a piece of infrastructure. The infrastructure will be operated by people who were not at any of the design meetings, in conditions the design assumed could be assumed away, regulated by institutions whose concerns are not the engineer's. The part the engineer is designing has to do its job inside all of that.

The question her training has prepared her to ask is whether the part meets its specifications. The question the systems tradition asks of the same part is what the part does to the whole it enters. The two questions are different. The first is the question whose answer she can be tested on. The second is the question whose answer the world will eventually deliver, in a form she may not be present to see.

## From optimisation to design

The systems tradition makes a distinction the engineer's training rarely emphasises. The distinction is between optimisation and design.

Optimisation is what the engineer does when the problem has been bounded. The variables to be optimised are specified. The constraints to be respected are known. The objective function — the thing one is trying to maximise or minimise — has been chosen. Inside the bounded problem, the engineer's training applies; calculus, linear programming, numerical methods, the mathematical techniques the discipline has accumulated since the eighteenth century all bear on the well-posed problem. The engineer's competence at optimisation is, by any historical measure, formidable. Most of what the modern world is built of was produced by engineers whose competence at optimisation was the load-bearing skill of the work.

Design is what the engineer does when the problem has not yet been bounded. The variables to be optimised have not been specified, because the question of which variables matter is itself part of what the design is for. The constraints have not been finalised, because some of them will be discovered only when a candidate design is examined. The objective function is contested, because different parties to the design care about different things, and the design is in part the negotiation among those cares. Inside the design problem, the engineer's training partly applies; the techniques of optimisation are still useful, but they have been demoted from the work to a subordinate role in it. The work is the design, and the design is what determines which optimisations are worth performing.

Russell Ackoff, who came to the systems tradition from operations research and saw the limits of his original training from the inside, made the distinction repeatedly. Ackoff's claim was that the engineering education of the twentieth century had trained engineers magnificently for optimisation and badly for design.[^1] The result, by the closing decades of the century, was a built world full of optimised parts that failed in their configurations: machines whose components met their specifications and whose interactions destroyed the systems they were supposed to constitute; infrastructures whose subsystems each ran efficiently and whose overall behaviour was unstable; institutions whose departments were each managed competently and whose collective performance steadily worsened. The pattern was not the failure of optimisation. The pattern was the absence of design.

The engineer's path, on Ackoff's account, is from optimisation to design. The path does not abandon optimisation. The optimisation of the part is still the engineer's work; the part still has to be made well. But the optimisation is now subordinate to a question the engineer's training did not prepare her to ask: what does the optimised part do to the whole the part is entering? The answer to that question is the design's, not the optimiser's. The competence to give the answer is the competence the next decades of engineering will most demand and most rarely supply.

## Petroski on failure

The engineer who knows what optimisation cannot deliver knows it because of failure. Henry Petroski's argument, in *To Engineer Is Human*, was that engineering knowledge advances by failures more reliably than by successes; that the working artefact is silent about its limits, and that the failed artefact is the only kind that speaks clearly about where the limits are.[^2]

A bridge that has stood for a century says nothing useful about the bridges next to it. It says only that the design has held, under conditions that have so far obtained. Whether the design will hold under conditions that have not yet obtained — winds the bridge has not yet seen, loads it has not yet borne, temperatures it has not yet been exposed to — the bridge cannot say. The successful bridge is the one whose tested envelope has not yet been exceeded. The boundary of the envelope is invisible from inside it.

A bridge that has fallen, by contrast, has located the boundary precisely. The conditions that produced the failure can be reconstructed; the assumptions in the original design that proved inadequate can be identified; the analysis the next bridge must include can be specified. The failed bridge has produced engineering knowledge of a kind no successful bridge could produce. Every working bridge designed thereafter incorporates the lesson of the failed one.

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge, which collapsed within months of its 1940 opening, has become a canonical illustration of the pattern Petroski described. The bridge had been designed to satisfy the static loading specifications a long-span suspension bridge of the period was expected to meet. It collapsed in winds well below the static load it had been designed for, because the designers had not considered an interaction the static analysis had no place for: a self-excited oscillation between wind and structure that the bridge could not damp. The failure was not a failure of the parts. The parts were each within their specifications. The failure was a failure of the configuration — the interaction between wind and bridge that the design had not contemplated.

The lesson, Petroski argued, was that engineering education and engineering practice should treat failure not as the disgrace the profession had often made of it but as the discipline's most reliable instructor. The successful design is provisional. Its success is silent about its boundary. The failed design is articulate; it says exactly where the boundary was, and what the next design must do to move it. An engineering culture that hides its failures, or treats them as the personal shame of the engineers responsible, is a culture in which the discipline cannot accumulate the knowledge it most needs.

The implication of Petroski's argument extended beyond pedagogy. The engineer working on a new design has, in the failures of previous designs, the most reliable record of what the new design's hidden assumptions are likely to be. The discipline of design begins with the question of which assumptions the design is implicitly making; the failures of the past are the catalogue of the assumptions previous designs made that turned out to be wrong. The engineer who has not studied the failures has not yet begun the work.

## Vincenti on what engineers know

Walter Vincenti, writing five years after Petroski, gave the historical complement to the argument. Vincenti's book asked what engineering knowledge actually consisted of — what engineers knew that scientists did not, what engineers knew that they could not justify by appeal to underlying physics, and what kind of knowledge engineering practice produced and accumulated over time.[^3]

His answer, drawn from a decade of close historical study of US aeronautical engineering between the world wars, was that engineering knowledge was a body of design practice rather than applied science. Engineers knew how to make things that worked. They sometimes knew, after the fact, why what worked worked. Their reliable knowledge — the knowledge that survived from one design generation to the next — was knowledge of design itself: of which configurations had been tried, of which had succeeded and failed, of which design moves were available to address which problems, of how the boundary conditions of a known design domain had been mapped.

Vincenti's distinction between *engineering knowledge* and *applied science* was not pedantic. The applied-science view of engineering had, throughout the twentieth century, encouraged the assumption that engineers were doing applied physics, and that engineering errors were therefore failures to apply the underlying physics correctly. Vincenti's historical evidence indicated otherwise. Engineers were doing something the underlying physics could not by itself produce: they were designing within configurations the physics did not by itself specify. The configurations were what made the artefact work or fail. The configurations were what the engineering tradition had accumulated knowledge of.

The implication, for the engineer asking what kind of knowledge her work was producing, was that her work was in the long tradition of design rather than the shorter one of applied science. The discipline she was extending was older than the physics it relied on. It had its own ways of accumulating knowledge — through the failure record Petroski had emphasised, through the working artefact, through the design tradition's stored memory of what had been tried — and those ways were the ones the discipline had to honour if it was to continue to learn.

## Perrow on the structural inevitability

The pattern Petroski had described as the engineer's reliance on the failure record received, in the same period, a structural diagnosis from a sociologist who was watching engineering from outside. Charles Perrow's central case was the 1979 partial meltdown at the Three Mile Island reactor unit. The accident proceeded by a sequence in which a small valve failure propagated, through the reactor's cooling and instrumentation, into a loss of coolant the operators were unable to diagnose because the displays they relied on showed them what the design had assumed would be the case rather than what was. The operators acted reasonably given what they were shown. What they were shown was wrong. The plant moved into a state the design had no clear representation of. The accident was not the failure of any single part. It was the system, as designed, doing what such a system would do given a small initiating disturbance.

*Normal Accidents* generalised the diagnosis. Perrow argued that some classes of system, by virtue of their structure, were going to produce accidents the design could not anticipate, and that those accidents were not user errors or freak occurrences but the system functioning as designed.[^4]

Two structural variables turned out to be doing the work. The first was the *coupling* of the system: the degree to which the parts were connected such that disturbance in one propagated rapidly and inflexibly to others. A tightly coupled system was one whose parts moved together; a loosely coupled system was one whose parts could absorb disturbance without immediately transferring it. The second was the *interaction* of the system: the degree to which the parts behaved in linear and predictable ways when they affected one another, versus the degree to which the interactions were complex — multi-causal, feedback-laden, unanticipated by the design. Three Mile Island had been tightly coupled and complexly interactive. So were most of the canonical accidents that came after it.

Where the coupling was tight and the interactions complex, Perrow argued, accidents that the design could not have anticipated were structurally inevitable. The combination produced a system in which a small disturbance, propagating rapidly through tight couplings, would interact with the system's other components in ways the original design had no model of. The resulting cascade was not the failure of any single part. It was the system's structural response to the disturbance, given the configuration the engineers had built. The accident was, in Perrow's terms, a *normal accident*: the kind of event that the system, as designed, would eventually produce.

The category of the normal accident was the engineering discipline's most important conceptual debt to a sociologist, because it relocated the burden of explanation from the failed component to the configuration the components were embedded in. After Perrow, an engineering investigation that concluded by identifying the operator who had made the wrong move, or the part that had failed, or the procedure that had been violated, was no longer adequate. The investigation had to ask why the configuration had been one in which the operator's move, the part's failure, or the procedure's violation was capable of producing the cascade. The configuration was where the design's responsibility now lay.

The implication for design was that some systems — the tightly-coupled, complexly-interactive ones — should not be built at all in their proposed forms, because no operational discipline would prevent the eventual accident. Other systems should be redesigned for looser coupling or simpler interactions, even at a cost in efficiency, because the tightly-coupled, complexly-interactive design carried structural risks the looser design would not. The engineer's task, after Perrow, was not to optimise the tightly-coupled, complexly-interactive system into safety; it was to design the system away from the configuration in which normal accidents were inevitable.

## Leveson on the absence of control

Nancy Leveson, working three decades after Perrow inside engineering itself, gave the systems-theoretic shift the technical apparatus that engineering practice had been missing. Her book proposed a framework she called STAMP — the Systems-Theoretic Accident Model and Processes — that recast safety as a property of the configuration rather than as the absence of identified component failures.[^5]

The traditional engineering approach to safety, on Leveson's account, had been a chain-of-events model. An accident was a chain in which a sequence of failures led to the bad outcome. The discipline of safety was therefore the discipline of identifying possible failures and breaking the chains: redundancy, fault tolerance, defence in depth. The approach worked tolerably for systems whose accidents really were chains of component failures. It worked poorly for the systems Perrow had named — the tightly-coupled, complexly-interactive ones — in which the accident emerged from interactions the chain-of-events model had no place for.

Leveson's reformulation drew on control theory. A system, on the systems-theoretic view, was a hierarchical control structure: each level of the system controlled the level below it by issuing commands and observing whether the constraints required for safe operation were being honoured. Safety was the system property of having those constraints enforced throughout the structure. An unsafe outcome was the result of a constraint that the control structure had failed to enforce. The accident was not a chain of component failures; it was a control failure — the absence, somewhere in the structure, of the control loop that was supposed to keep the system inside its safe envelope.

The shift had practical consequences. An accident investigation conducted under STAMP would not stop at the failed component or the operator who had taken the wrong action. It would ask, of every level of the system's control structure, why the constraint that should have prevented the accident had not been enforced: why the operator had not had the information needed to enforce it, why the operator's supervisors had not noticed that the information was missing, why the regulatory body had not required that the information be provided, and so on up the structure. The accident's explanation lay in the absence of control across multiple levels, not in the failure of any one component. The redesign that the investigation called for was a redesign of the control structure, not a replacement of the failed part.

For the engineer working on a new system, STAMP changed the design discipline. The engineer was now designing not just the components and their interconnections but the control structure that would enforce the constraints under which the system would be safe. The control structure was as much an engineering artefact as the physical system. Its design required understanding what could disturb the system, what constraints had to be enforced to keep the system inside its safe envelope under those disturbances, and what information had to flow at what rate to which agent for the constraints to be enforceable in practice. The engineer's competence was now control-theoretic as well as component-theoretic. The systems tradition's central abstraction — feedback in service of stability — was now the engineer's working tool.

## Wiener and Beer on the engineer's organisation

The control-theoretic shift Leveson formalised had been prefigured, decades earlier, by the cybernetic engineers. Norbert Wiener's *Cybernetics* had given the engineering discipline a vocabulary for systems whose behaviour was constituted by their own control. Stafford Beer's *Brain of the Firm* extended that vocabulary into the design of organisations.[^6]

Wiener's central observation was that the line between organism and machine, when one considered the role of feedback in producing purposive behaviour, was not where the engineering tradition had drawn it. The engineering tradition had treated machines as the things one designed and organisms as the things one studied. Wiener proposed that the engineer's question — how feedback in a configuration produces stable, purposive behaviour — was the same question regardless of whether the configuration was made of metal or of nerves. The mathematics was shared. The principles were shared. The discipline was the same discipline.

This had two consequences for engineering. The first was that engineers could now work on a class of artefact — the controlled, feedback-driven, purposive system — that the engineering tradition before Wiener had treated as exotic. After Wiener, such systems were the engineering tradition's central case. Servomechanisms, automatic gun-laying, thermostats, autopilots, and eventually computers and networks were the engineering objects of the second half of the twentieth century, and all of them were designed under the cybernetic vocabulary Wiener had given the discipline.

The second consequence, slower to mature, was that the cybernetic vocabulary applied to systems that were not made of metal. An organisation, on Beer's extension, was a controlled, feedback-driven, purposive system whose components were people and procedures rather than valves and circuits. The engineering tradition's competence in designing such systems applied to organisations as well, once one had accepted that the designed object included its own control. Beer proposed that the design of organisations — what configuration of communication channels, decision points, and feedback loops would produce stable behaviour against the disturbances the organisation would face — was an engineering problem of a kind the discipline had not yet recognised it was equipped to address.[^7]

The cybernetic engineers were prefiguring what Leveson would later codify. The engineer's object was a configuration that included its own control. The engineer's design problem was the design of that control. The engineering tradition, when it was at its best, had always been doing this; the cybernetic vocabulary was what made the practice articulate. Leveson's STAMP and Beer's viable systems differed in their idiom and in the systems they had been developed against, but the structural commitment was the same: the configuration that includes its own feedback is the configuration the engineer is now designing, and the design is incomplete until the feedback is part of it.

## What changes when the engineer asks the systems question

The engineer who has absorbed the systems tradition's questions does her work differently from the engineer who has not. The differences are not stylistic. They run through every step of the design.

The first difference is in the criteria the design must meet. The engineer who is designing for the part's specifications is asking whether the part will function inside its specified envelope. The engineer who is designing for the configuration is asking what range of disturbances the part must remain stable against, what boundary conditions the part must not violate, and what information must flow to which agent for the configuration to remain inside the envelope of safe behaviour. The first set of criteria is local. The second set is structural; it cannot be assessed by examining the part alone.

The second difference is in the collaborations the design requires. The engineer who is designing for the part's specifications can do most of the work alone; her collaborations are with the engineers immediately upstream and downstream in the design chain. The engineer who is designing for the configuration is in collaboration with the operators who will run the system, the institutions that will regulate it, the maintenance personnel who will keep it functional over decades, and the people whose lives will be conducted inside or near it. The configuration is constituted by what these other parties bring to it. The engineer's design cannot be complete without them.

The third difference is in the failure modes the design tries to anticipate. The engineer who is designing for the part's specifications anticipates the failure modes of the part: the wear patterns, the load excursions, the operating temperatures outside the design range. The engineer who is designing for the configuration anticipates the configuration's failure modes: the cascading failures Perrow described, the absences of control Leveson named, the misalignments between system and operator that have produced most of the canonical accidents of the past century. The configuration's failure modes are not the union of the parts' failure modes. They are a different set, identifiable only at the level of the whole.

The fourth difference is in what the engineer has to know. The engineer who is designing for the part's specifications has to know the materials, the loads, the operating conditions, and the relevant calculations. The engineer who is designing for the configuration has to know all of that and also the structure of feedback in the configuration she is designing into, the institutions that will operate around the configuration, the time-scales over which the configuration will be expected to function, the disturbances against which it must remain stable, and the human and organisational behaviours the configuration will need to accommodate. The expansion is large. It is not optional. The configurations the next decades of engineering will produce — the energy infrastructures, the digital networks, the autonomous systems, the embedded artificial intelligences — are configurations of the kind Perrow and Leveson identified as requiring the systems-theoretic discipline. An engineer trained only for the part will be, in those configurations, structurally inadequate to the work.

## Closing

The next decades' best engineers will be system designers. The claim is not that part specialism is obsolete. The part still has to be made well; the optimisation is still a load-bearing competence; the engineer who cannot calculate the part's behaviour cannot design the configuration the part is going to enter. The claim is that the configuration is what the engineer is now being asked to produce, and the configuration cannot be produced by the optimisation of its parts alone.

Engineering education will eventually catch up with this. The institutions will be slow, because the optimisation curriculum is the one the institutions have built over a century, and the design curriculum that the systems tradition implies is one the institutions have not yet built. In the interval — which is the interval the engineers of the next decades will work inside — the systems-theoretic discipline will have to be acquired the way Petroski recommended the discipline of failure be acquired: by attention to the failure record, by study of the configurations that have collapsed and the configurations that have endured, by close reading of the engineers who saw before others where the discipline was going.

The engineers who saw it earliest were not the most prominent of their generations. Wiener was a mathematician working on problems engineers were uncomfortable claiming as theirs; Beer worked on organisations that engineers had not previously thought of as engineering objects; Perrow was a sociologist whose categorisations engineers initially resisted; Leveson developed STAMP at the boundary between safety engineering and systems theory, where neither discipline's prestige was on her side. What each of them saw was that the engineering object was changing. The configurations engineering had been asked to produce were no longer the bounded, optimisable, component-decomposable objects the discipline's original training had been built around. They were configurations of the kind every chapter of this book has been describing: emergent, feedback-laden, embedded in institutions, dependent on operators and maintainers and regulators the engineer would never meet. The discipline that could produce such configurations responsibly was the discipline the systems tradition had been developing since the middle of the twentieth century. The engineers who learned what it was teaching became engineers of the next discipline. The engineers who did not are responsible for the configurations whose accidents fill the standard histories of engineering failure.

What the engineer asks, when she has fully absorbed the question, is no longer whether the design works. She asks what the design does to the whole it enters. The first question is the question of optimisation. The second is the question of design. The systems tradition's discipline is the discipline of asking the second.
